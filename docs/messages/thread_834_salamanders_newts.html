Hoopoe,

You've seen your share of rough-skinned newts in water or on land, right?
Newts are salamanders, but are generally distinguished from other salamanders by
their rough-textured skins. Rough-skinned newts are the bold ones that
saunter about in broad daylight. You know, the ones with the orange bellies. (Ooh
boy, don't get a naturalist started--oops, too late.) Here's the reason for
the boldness/daylight strolls, the colorful bellies AND the bumpy skin: if you
annoy these newts, the bumps can secrete a highly-toxic poison--as their
bright bellies signal. Luckily, they seem to be pretty easygoing critters-- and
if you get one cranky enough, it'll arc up both its head & tail to better show
off its belly and let you know you you're messing with. Their toxin can't
penetrate human skin, but it's a smart idea to wash/rinse your hands after
handling one--even a well-adjusted, non-cranky one--since you don't want to injest
any of the stuff by accident (via your sandwich or whatever). There is a
reported case of campers who died after inadvertantly boiling a newt in their
coffee pot that had been left out overnight. But generations of West Coast
schoolkids (and plenty of us adults too) have handled zillions of these things
without keeling over, so there's no need to be overly worried about holding one,
either.

These newts spend most of their time on land--Wild Child picked up 8 or so
during our recent walk at Milo McIver--and will head for water only around
mating time--about Dec thru July (depending on elevation, etc).

Okay, okay, I'll stop now. (But if you want to know who their only predator
is, let me know!)
