From: Clio Mouse (cliomouse@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-05-28 22:33:45 UTC-07:00
Hi, there.

(Sorry if this gets sent twice...connection problems...)

Taking over for the week...

We've discussed how far you will go for a box in terms of how many miles. Now let's discuss how far you WON'T go for a box.

We all know (I think) to "leave no trace," but there are some gray areas...and some tempting boxes. National parks, private property...one step off the path won't leave a trace... If we never planted any boxes on property where people might in any way be displeased to find them, there would be a heck of a lot fewer boxes, which means some people are "breaking the rules." (But nobody mention any names, here! That's not my intention!) I know we don't want to admit when we've broken rules, but I'm sure we have all "slipped" every now and again and even thought of planting someplace verboten or simply stepped on something we shouldn't have.

Additionally, some boxes can require a lot of you. Certain skills (i.e. rock climbing) or simply a long hike can put it out of your range. Sometimes the way these things are mentioned in the clues, sometimes not. And sometimes you get to know that somebody else's "moderate difficulty" is not exactly your idea of "moderate." Or maybe you're really looking for those long ones (mentioning names here: RYAN...haha...) and the short hikes just don't do it for you.

So what do people put in clues that deters you? Hike is too tough? Hike is too easy? Not an interesting park? Location seems a little (or a lot) over the line? When have you thought, "You know, this is just not worth it..." and gave up the possibility for a stamp?

Inquiring minds and all that...

ClioMouse

I hate it when I have to turn back from a box. One of those happened not to

From: Jana J. Riska (jjcadenza@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-05-29 10:56:50 UTC-06:00
I already shared my Paris boxing experience--not giving up on one after
several other failed exploits, which left me climbing a fence and
trespassing onto an abandoned railway line then being threatened by some
local men. That was my "shouldn't have done it and never will again"
experience (although it makes a fun story now that I'm okay). That same
week the same planter tried to get me to climb a city building wall
Spiderman-style and to climb up on a stone wall that surrounded private
property only to shimmy up a bit of lightpost. I had the sense to say no to
both of those. It drove me crazy to stare at the spots the boxes were
hidden only to walk away, but I didn't feel like risking my neck. I've
decided not to look for any of that particular boxer's boxes again.

I think for the most part though most letterboxers in my area have been
pretty responsible. When I find myself debating if I should get in a risky
spot or go out on a precipice or something of that sort, I start retracing
my steps to see if I've misread the clue. I find myself more and more
getting a sense of "I don't think they would have meant for us to do that"
when I'm in the wrong spot. Luckily very few boxes have been more than a
few steps off-trail either, so I haven't had to debate much over if I'm
hurting habitat.

My real temptation as far as planting goes though has been national parks.
I've managed to not plant there even though I live a stone's throw from some
of the most amazing national parks. Finding boxes others have left there on
the other hand . . . You don't have to twist my arm too hard.

Cadenza

From: tamaialden (tamaialden@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-05-29 17:11:17 UTC
The long hikes are pretty much out of the question for me right now. My kids
range in age from four to nine, and I don't like to go boxing (or hiking) alone. I
need to share the thrill with someone. And I'm not in condition to do the
marathon ones, although I'm working on it.

I also generally have avoided the more adult themed boxes, because I always
have at least one kid with me. So the beer and wine stamps, for example, are
ones I don't have (although I'd really like to go get the Edgefield boxes).

And I have to admit, I often won't go out of my way to search for boxes that
others haven't been able to find recently (marked "attempted" on the LBNA
site). I'm spoiled to have scores of great boxes within easy driving range, so
that I tend to stick with ones there's a decent chance of finding (fair-weather
boxer! ;-). We did, however, find "See Spot" a couple of months ago, and
when I went to log it at the site, realized that several people had been
unsuccessful. There's a lesson in there somewhere!

-Logos

From: funhog1 (funhog@pacifier.com) | Date: 2006-05-29 18:42:21 UTC
Don't let those "attempted" notices discourage you. I have several
boxes that are in decidedly good health that baffle boxers on a
regular basis. Then there are those pesky boxes that go missing only
to reappear later or have been moved to a slightly different location.

I won't go for boxes that are in fragile places, like steep, erosive
hillsides or have tons of people around. There are a couple of them
that have killed me to miss but I just couldn't in good conscience go
for them. I won't letterbox in an urban park like Forest Park alone
but have no problem hiking solo in more remote places. Funhog

From: SloMo (SloMo86@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-06-05 16:03:36 UTC-07:00
You're absolutely right, Funhog. The same has happens with our boxes.
Also, I can relate with Logos. I wish there was a way to know which ones
are really missing. This last week I've spent hours going through the list
of Portland boxes which prompted me to start marking the attempted boxes
"ignore". Now, I'm not complaining about too many boxes - keep 'em
coming!!

Figuring out the box locations must be easier for those familiar with
Portland. We try to figure out all the boxes in a particular area before
the hunt. It's easy for Gresham, Estacada, etc as they are short lists
compared to Portland. Well, you know, for Portland you have to go through
the whole list! Guess that's why we've barely hit the Portland boxes. By
the time we get around to Portland again the list is old. It would be
pretty nifty if the listings included the sector - such as "Portland - SE".
A few boxes do include their location and I find that very helpful.

Anyone have any advice to help this country bumpkin navigate the city please
write me!

SloMo
