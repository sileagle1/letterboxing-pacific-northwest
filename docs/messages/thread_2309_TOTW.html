Der Mad Stamper's Spirit of Dartmoor is a classic. Delightfully fun to
work on, delightfully fun to get, and not only is it in the Spirit of
Dartmoor, but is also the inspiration of the Pacific Northwest. That's
what first got me started with multi-color stamps which, at the time
was so new to everyone, that I created the "Stamping with Style"
tutorial. I used to link to it from the stamps I planted where I
recommended using multiple colors, though now it no longer seems
necessary since everyone does multi-colored stamps.

An oldie but goodie. =)

From: funhog1 (funhog@pacifier.com) | Date: 2005-12-05 01:55:22 UTC
One of my personal favorites for a rainy day is Jay's Toolbox in
Troutdale! Although you might need an umbrella to get the box, it is
not a terribly long walk and you can bring it back to your car for
stamping. The two times I've been there, that's just what we did. The
process can get pretty involved as the number of stamps grows and grows.

I think this box is one of the cleverest innovations to hit the scene.
Since its debut, there have been a couple of other versions created
using a similar concept but this is the grand daddy. I look forward to
another visit soon to see what new tools have been added to the
collection.

As for the Spirit of Dartmoor, get crackin' on that one if you haven't
solved it yet. Trust me, it will come in handy at a later date... Funhog

couldn't find--heaven only knows. I remember a certain box of Ryan's in
this area that I read way to much into the clue and unfortunately some
markers in the trail backed it up. Every time I decided that there was no

the place. (Isn't it funny how your mind can twist things to fit a clue

looked like any hiker, I thought, I was standing there with my pack on
looking at a printout of a clue. I was miles off course. A van pulled up,

hiked. I about fell over when the guy stuck his head out and asked, "excuse
me, but are you a letterboxer?" Keep in mind, there aren't really other
letterboxers in my area at all. I have NEVER had a run in on the trail! I
about fell over. When I asked how in the world he knew to ask me that, he
(AZRoadie) said "because yesterday I combed this whole mountain and found
myself standing in the exact same spot you are at now, looking at a clue
with the exact same confused look on my face!" I was laughing so hard! We
spent the rest of the afternoon searching together. We were pretty
embarrassed when we realized that we had put way too much into the clue and
had gone right past it before. Boy, did we ever literally do happy dances
when we finally did find the box!

Well, probably the thing that most dissuades me from some boxes is if the
location is just too much of a bushwhack. We were once on a trail where a
letterbox was planted off-trail, steeply uphill, in fallen timber and
plants, in an area posted prominently that trails had been extensively
damaged by cutting-off trails, and there was a fine if you were caught doing
it. We decided to leave that one lie - not because of the fine, but because
I have a daughter that I want to learn respect for the parks, and I'd not
like her to think it's acceptable to trample the vegetation and make a
social trail for a letterbox, even if we hiked 10 miles to get there.

We also looked for another that I think wasn't an off-trail bushwhack when
it was planted, but certainly is now, so we left it alone and enjoyed the
location instead.

I've done several where I get to the locale, and the box is obviously in the
middle of a thicket of undergrowth. No way I'd do that.

I can be a bit of a klutz, so I tend to avoid ones that are red-flagged as
"don't take your children this is down a steep hill, on the edge of a bluff,
and you have to hang upside-down in a bat cave to get it."

Long hikes don't dissuade me, but high-elevation-gain hikes do, because I'm
simply not fit enough to attempt them yet. But they're on my list for when
I am! Then, maybe I'll see some more Green Tortugas!

My 2 cents,
