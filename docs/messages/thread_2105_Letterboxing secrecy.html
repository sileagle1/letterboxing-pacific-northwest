Letterboxing secrecy

From: Kris Leibrand (bookwormlb@comcast.net) | Date: 2005-06-20 15:41:52 UTC-07:00
Seahunter & I went down to the beach this weekend for a lovely stay in the Astoria area. Unfortunately, we didn't get to do any letterboxing because I brought the clues for all of Oregon and Washington EXCEPT the Oregon coast clues (boo hoo). While I was at Fort Stevens complaining that I just KNEW there was a letterbox somewhere in the park and wondering if I could possibly find it without clues (I'm nothing if not hopeful), Seahunter pointed out that there was a letterboxer asking someone for directions over by the museum.

Thinking he was just trying to get me to shut up about not bringing the clues, I casually strolled over to check and found a letterboxer asking one of the park employees to help her solve the clues to the box! She had her clues spread out on the table and they were reading them together. I couldn't figure out whether to be appalled or impressed by this. What do you guys think? I always thought that you were supposed to be casual and unobtrusive in hunting for boxes. This really seems to go against the unofficial letterboxers creed.

Bookworm

Re: Letterboxing secrecy

From: erfellie (hemenekawan@hotmail.com) | Date: 2005-06-21 00:37:23 UTC
In desperate times I have asked others for help in locating
something, like a statue in the clues, for example, but if the people
start asking questions, I just casually mention something about
scavenger hunt, if they get more pressing about it, I usually just
say its geocaching, since more people tend to understand that, but
even at this point I wont say much about it unless I can see the
person is genuinely interested and doesnt seem much like a vandal. I
did run across one couple a while back, on a very long hike and when
i had the LB and all its contents spread across my lap and the rocks
in the middle of nowhere they did ask me and I did tell them what it
was because they seemed genuinely interested. After all, they were
hiking way out in the middle of nowhere, like me, and obviously
enjoyed the sport and they seemed very sincere about it and I felt ok
telling them about it, and whaddya know, some time later I actually
saw there names and a stamped image in another LB I found! So I guess
they did enjoy the sport.
I have noticed sometimes asking park staff for directions and such
isnt a good idea, for example in a state park in Troutdale, OR we
talked to one park staff (used the word scavenger hunt) and he got
really angry and started yelling at us about "da*n treasure hunters,
dont put your stuff here!" but then there are some park staff that
are respectful of the sport, like I think the staff at Silver Falls
are, it brings more people to the area. So I do try and be as
secretive as possible, but sometimes we all need a little help in
locating a landmark or something so I dont think its like cheating to
ask for help, but you should be very selective in who you talk to and
what you tell them. Thats my 2 cents anyways :o)

From: melanie (maiden1974@verizon.net) | Date: 2005-06-20 18:02:40 UTC-07:00
I agree that it's all in how you do it. I've been known to ask park staff if
they know where I might find a "xyz", but I never tell them what I'm doing,
and NEVER show them the clues unless the clues tell me the staff know what's
happening.

Letterboxing is supposed to be secretive, I like it that way and I hope
things like you ran into Bookworm aren't happening very often or I'm afraid
more boxes will start coming up missing.

Maiden
