>
> Question for the group:
> When does a letterboxer advance from newbie to experienced?

Who knows? After 2-1/2 years of letterboxing, some 900+ finds, some
190+ planted, etc, we still learn something new EVERY time we're out
there! Then, that learning curve increases EXPONENTIALLY at every
gathering we're able to attend! It's incredible what remains
undiscovered about this hobby.

HISTORY:

First, the history of it (Dartmoor & US) is rather intriguing in
itself--even just the fact that just a few folks interested in the
Apr 98 Smithsonian Mag article have been able to create such a
letterboxing phenomenon in this country is incredible!

MECHANICS OF FINDING A LETTERBOX:

Then, the mechanics of letterboxing itself--from orienteering (which
I do for a living with my job--and I still learn something new
while letterboxing), to following directions, to deciphering clues,
to actually finding & carefully replanting/recamouflaging well
hidden items in the woods, to learning some history and visiting a
place you otherwise would never know about had it not been for
somebody else participating in the hobby and planting a letterbox
out there to seek out. Anybody familiar with the LBNA FAQs or a
similar letterboxing Code of Conduct out there?

MECHANICS OF PLANTING A LETTERBOX:

Creating a unique letterbox--which includes a neat stamp (I
personally prefer handcarved to storebought but I don't care
how 'rustic' the handcarved ones are--I treasure the 'effort & time'
somebody invests into the project over the final product), a great
clue (love it when history is woven into the clue or the verbage is
able to teach something new, regardless of subject, to the finder)
and a visit to either a beautiful or historically rich area. We
even love 'kitschy' type places. Just find a unique venue in which
to plant the letterbox. How many folks out there aren't aware that
the little 'bulb' end of the Speedball carving tool actually comes
off so you can store your carving nibs within? What containers do
you use--tupperware, rubbermaid, vhs cassette cases, altoid tins,
film canisters, magnets, velcro, baby sock christmas ornaments, fake
book, puzzle boxes in a cafe? Do you use pre-made notebooks, hand-
crafted logbooks, scrolls, hand-bound fabric covered books, etc to
include in your boxes?

MECHANICS OF CLUE WRITING:

Sometimes in clues, less is more. Other times more is better. Just
create something interesting & unique. Puzzles, cryptogram,
anagrams, foreign language, substition style codes--word stories
with clue bits hidden within, etc--something fun & informative
always does well. Photographs with no text, pictures with embedded
text--fake websites, GPS coordinates, the ideas are limitless.
Where do your ideas for writing clues come from? Newspaper articles,
historical society publications, bits of info found in 'fine print'
in museum displays,blurbs on trail kiosks, etc? Sometimes clue
distribution/location is a challenge and art all unto itself. Clues
to letterboxes are EVERYWHERE. Many clues are embedded in messages--
some are links to files, some hidden in photos/movies/lyrics to
songs, some show up in your postal box--you just never know. How do
you distribute clues to your boxes out there? Will it be a
straightforward clue or a mystery clue?

MECHANICS OF STAMP CARVING:

Where do you come up with your ideas for new, unique stamps? Clip
art, an idea that popped into your head, doodles on a restaurant
napkin, images you see on billboards/travel brochures/yahoo ads?
What carving medium do you use? School erasers, Mars carve,
speedball pink/white crumbly stuff, PZ Kut, dollar store erasers, 3-
D puzzle foam pieces, etc? How do you transfer the images?
Acetone, iron/heat transfer, pen/pencil transfer? What carving
tools do you use? Xacto knives, Speed ball linoleum carving tools,
scissors tips, hypodermic needle tips, wood carving tools, dremel
tools, dental tools, etc? How do you hold the tools, what do you
place the carving medium on, do you turn the medium or turn the tool
as you carve? What techniques do you use--Van Gogh-esque, Picasso-
esque, Edvard Muench-esque, Grandma Moses-esque, etc? Gouges,
straight lines, pecks, backhanded, cross hatching, shading, bevelled-
edges, feathered edging, etc? Lettering--free hand, newsprint
fonts, Old Gothic, wing-dings?

I'd say there's always room for improvement. Probably to transition
from newbie to experienced would involve participating in at least
each 'facet' of letterboxing for a little bit--i.e. to find some
letterboxes, create & plant some letterboxes of your own, meet some
fellow letterboxers for exchanges, solve/plant a few mystery boxes
in your area. I'm convinced that folks really must plant a
letterbox of their own to really 'appreciate' the work/effort that
goes into some of the other planted letterboxes out there. There's
just something about going through the process--logistically and
emotionally that really teaches folks to appreciate the treasures
they find planted by others out there. That whole 'letting go of
your child into the big, bad world' feeling one experiences when
they plant that first letterbox helps teach folks to truly
appreciate & respect other boxes already out in the world. Only
then, will folks understand the 'anger/sadness' that planters
experience when beloved boxes go missing.

So, what it boils down to is that there is no official 'threshold'
for the 'transition' from newbie to experienced boxer out there but
rather it's just sort of an evolution as folks are exposed to new &
different things about the hobby along the way. Believe me, there's
always something else out there to be 'discovered'. Then, add some
new inventions along the way--Postal Letterboxes, Hitchhikers,
Cooties with some of the older convetions such as Personal
Travellers-- there's always room to grow!!! As you can see, the
possibilities are limitless!!!

Just be sure to slow down & savor the experience along the way!
