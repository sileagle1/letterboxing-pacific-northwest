From: Amanda Arkebauer (samanark@yahoo.com) | Date: 2003-05-30 16:49:00 UTC
--- In LbPNW@yahoogroups.com, "wingnut_lb" wrote:
> Just curious, how do you all decide where to leave your boxes?

Basically, I use all of the techniques that you mentioned. I will
drive by a park and think that it needs a letterbox, or I will see a
park on the map that doesn't have a letterbox in it and decide that I
will carve and plant one there. Sometimes, I have an idea for a stamp
and then I try to find a park to hide it in. Ryan's African Safari
series is a good example of that. He carved the stamps first. African
animals...and then decided to hide them all over the Columbia River
Gorge. Sometimes you hear the name of a park or a trail that just
begs for a stamp. The Shy Bear trail at Cougar Mountain was one of
those. I just had to carve a "shy bear" and hide it on that trail.
Do
> you search out a park first then go carve a box for a predetermined
> location?
--sometimes, --This way you can put the name of the park or trail on
your stamp (if it's NOT a mystery)-- or if you are hiding the box by
a weird looking tree, your stamp could be of that weird-looking tree.

Do you know of a park, carve a box, then go scout out a
> location when it is done?

--sometimes, like if you want to do a series of stamps about Fly-
Fishing and you think you might hide it in a park where people fly-
fish. So you carve your stamps and then you go out and try to find
places to hide them. Sometimes you find that the first park you
thought of won't work and you have to hide the boxes somewhere else.

How do you find your parks? Off a list
> somewhere like THPRD? From previous visits?

Yes and Yes. I look at maps, I do internet searches, I drive around!
Say that I want to hide a stamp of a beaver in a park that has beaver
dams in it. I do an internet search of local parks and beaver dams.
Bingo. Maybe I was in a local park hunting for a letterbox and I
found a great spot to hide another letterbox. I'll go home and carve
a stamp to hide at that park too.

How far do you plant and
> travel to maintain them?
>
Well, I plant extremely far away and I don't really maintain them.
Since I am a flight attendant, there is a chance that I will get back
to maintain a box on the East Coast. But sometimes I have trouble
maintaining boxes right here in my own back yard. I have boxes up in
Bellingham, but if they go missing, it might be a year before I get
back up there to fix that. Sometimes you just have to retire the
box. I had a box in Montreal, Canada that went missing. I just
retired it from the LBNA list because I will not be back to replace
it and there is no one nearby who can do it for me.

If you place them far
> away, do you have a friend close to it go and keep check on it for
> you?

This works sometimes. I have had folks replace boxes for me. For
example, Candlelight replaced one of my Fort Worden boxes before the
park pulled them all. I will not be getting back to Fort Worden
anytime soon, so those boxes will be retired for quite awhile. (we
have tried to get the park there to release the boxes to Candlelight,
but they want to see me in person)


Could you place a far away box and make a note on the clues
> that it won't be maintained and it's "finders luck"?
This is done all the time! Your boxes are art that you give away --
you don't really "own" them once you release them into the wild. They
might go missing, they might be vandalized. But if they bring a few
folks some happiness, then they have done their job. Sometimes you
just have to let them go.

--Amanda from Seattle

How do I decide where to plant? Well...You can do it either way, carve
the stamp for a certain place and hope there is somewhere to place it.
Or scout it out first, find the perfect spot, come home and carve it and
then go back and place it.

An example of the first, where I carved a stamp and then went and hoped
there was a spot for it was Colliding Rivers in Douglas County, OR. The
stamp I carved, I think, is creative and beautiful. Imagine my dismay
when I went to the area I wanted to plant it and found no safe or decent
haven for my baby! Eventually, I, and a bunch of my cool cousins, went
across the road and found a spot for it. As far as I know it remains
there to this day, which surprises me since it seemed like the area was
prime real estate for underage drinking and mayhem. The other stamp in
the area, Wolf Creek Falls, that I thought I placed in a very nice
hiding place, went missing almost from the get-go, I believe. No one,
even the most diligent of letterboxers, has found this box and I think
there was some landslide activity that may have buried it. I have not
been back to Glide to check on it since I placed it. But I always like
to make my clues easy so people can find my boxes, so it must be gone.

A recent example of my scoping out a park and a great hiding spot before
carving the stamp was Canby Ferry at Molalla River State Park in Canby.
I found a wonderful little hidey hole and it remains there as we speak.

Scouting things out and then placing is probably more realistic, but
sometimes another area letterboxer may have had the same idea and what
you thought was your spot is already occupied with a letterbox that has
yet to be posted!!! That happened to me when I went to place boxes for
our summer event at Upper Macleay Park in Portland last August. There
was a perfect letterboxing hideaway and Thom Cheney had a wonderful
stamp already waiting there. Just goes to show...GREAT MINDS THINK
ALIKE. HA! HA!

As for replacing missing stamps, I think it's an excellent idea,
especially if you can get a copy of the original and just re-carve it.
Ryan has done this for Pony Tail and I'd like to do it for Jackon Bottom
Wetlands, but I never did get the original one in the first place
(anyone out there have a copy of it and want to send it along to me?)

There's my two cents worth. The advice is as good as you paid for, but
I do actually have a little experience at this sort of thing, so I'd say
it's pretty good.

Best regards,

Amanda Briles

I usually just hide a letterbox because I'm hiking around somewhere and there aren't any to look for. That's my USUAL mode of operation, though.

In other cases, I've found the spot first and carved a stamp especially for it. (Usually while on the trail at the hiding spot--but not always.)

Sometimes I carve stamps without a clue as to where they'll go, but I'll pick out whatever is most appropriate for a given location from my stamp collection.

If you hide enough boxes, you'll eventually do all the possibilities.

The Temple of Terror box I was inspired to hide after I visited the location once, and later carved a stamp and went back to hide it.

The African Safari Series I started carving while in Africa, and decided to hide them throughout the Columbia River Gorge.

The Around the World in 80 Days box was inspired by the stamp I carved. I saw the picture and wanted to carve it, and the clue was inspired by the stamp.

All of the lighthouse boxes I carved
before I went knowing I'd be driving down Highway 101 and figured I'd visit the places along the way.

The Land of Long Ago was inspired while driving down Highway 101 and I HAD to hide a box when I found the place. I carved that stamp on the trail at the bridge I mention in the clue since I hadn't planned on hiding one earlier.

Sometimes the generic stamp I carved becomes inspiration for a rather elaborate clue such as Chipmunk of Terror.

It takes all types!
