From: funhog1 (funhog@pacifier.com) | Date: 2005-01-13 23:20:55 UTC

Hi there! I have used both PZ Cut and Mastercarve. I find that the PZ cut holds fine
detail much better but is tougher to carve. Mastercarve is softer and I find it is usually
easier for beginners to work with. This material also comes in two thicknesses. I
usually use the 1/2 inch variety not the 3/4 inch.

In the field, I find that it is often difficult to get a good impression of a large stamp
made with the thinner PZ cut or Speedy Stamp unless it has a backing. Unbacked, the
thin media also I also have the added problem of my inky fingers touching the page
of my logbook and leaving unwanted marks. I'm not the tidiest hog on the block.

Honestly, I don't think which carving block you use. Just keep practicing. I have seen
fantastic stamps carved on everything from wine corks to Pink Pearl erasers. Practice,
good lighting and magnification will do wonders! Enjoy, Funhog

Folks use a variety of tools including an X-acto knife and Speedball lino gouges.
Several of the finest carvers I know use tools made from hypodermic needles, a sort
of micro blade. When I first started making stamps, I used the gouges exclusively but
over time found that I preferred an X-acto with a #11 tip.

Here again, I don't think there is any one tool that's the 'right' one. Pandora's
beautifully detailed stamps are made with gouges, Alafair's with needle tools and Der
Mad Stamper's with X-acto blades. Experiment and you will find your favorite, too.

Hi, Kim!

Welcome to the game!

Strangely enough, I actually use an xacto knife because I manage to
get fine details with it that I can't get with other tools!

Well, I can get fine detail with one of those homemade hypodermic
needle tools, but I don't have enough patience to use something that
small exclusively.

I like the xacto because it allows me to get into sharp corners, like
when I'm carving letters, with great precision and control. As
Funhog suggests, a little bit of magnification can be a great help!
Then, without changing tools, I can remove huge chunks of rubber
along the outside of the carving.

But of course, I've been carving erasers with an xacto knife for 22
years now, so you might say I've kind of gotten accustomed to it by
now. That doesn't mean that it's right for everyone.

I have compared techniques with several other experienced carvers,
and I truly believe there is no right and wrong. For instance, since
Pandora has responded to this thread, and is a fantastic carver, I
will use her as an example. I watched her recently as she tried her
hand at carving with an xacto knife for the first time. She looked
as awkward as a duck in snowboots. (Sorry, Pandy, but you know it's
true.) She managed to produce an acceptable (though simple) carving,
but I could tell she felt handicapped by the unfamiliar tool.

However, when Pandora puts a lino blade to the rubber, it's like
watching a gold-medal figure skater on ice! By the time she
completes her carving, I feel compelled to throw roses at her feet!

On the other hand, when I pick up a Speedball lino blade, I feel like
I'm playing piano with mittens on. Nothing turns out the way I want
it to!

So, to re-iterate what Funhog and Iron`Bear have said... experiment.
Try your hand at several different tools. Switch back and forth and
find out what tool works best for you at each stage of the carving
process. Then, when you find something that feels comfortable for
you, stick with it, keep practicing, and don't let anyone tell you
that you're doing it wrong.

If you mess up every now and then, don't sweat it! Throw your
disaster in a plastic container, hide it under a rock, and call it a
letterbox! Once in a while, you'll get everything just right and
you'll love your carving so much you won't be able to part with it.
That's cool, too. Bring it to the next gathering and show it off.

The best tip I have for you is to try holding your carving tool in
one place and move the rubber instead. This technique has always
worked well for me, and I've seen Pandora and many others doing the
same thing. It seems strange at first, but after a while it becomes
second nature. For the record, I tend to grip my xacto knife very
close to the blade (too close, sometimes, as the tiny scars in my
thumb will attest). I think this plays a somewhat of a role in my
ability to carve with precision.

Through the years, I've carved in just about every material
imaginable. Speedy-Cut, EZ Cut, PZ Kut, Mastercarve, plumber's
gasket, cork, foam, art gum erasers, rubber pencil erasers (green and
pink), ink erasers, polymer erasers, Fimo clay, potatoes... you name
it.

For years, my favorite material has been Pink Pearl pencil erasers. I
like them because I tend to carve small, detailed stamps, and the
Pink Pearls are firm and durable. However, they are somewhat porous,
requiring a heavy coating of ink to produce a good imprint, and they
only come in one size: small.

As a result, I am one of the many carvers who has gravitated towards
PZ Kut. It comes in large sheets, it is firm and durable like a Pink
Pearl, and it is smooth enough to produce a good print with a
moderate coating of ink. Mastercarve is easier to carve, but
detailed carvings tend to be very fragile, and if you press too hard
when you print with them, they will smoosh and smear.

But I think it's fun to switch things up a little every now and then.
