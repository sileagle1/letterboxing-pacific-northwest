From: rscarpen (RiskyNil@pocketmail.com) | Date: 2003-10-28 08:08:02 UTC
I don't carry a first aid kit for day hikes. I figure things like
bandaids can wait until after I get back to my car. At least I've
never heard of anyone dieing from not getting a bandaid on quick
enough.

While backpacking on the AT I did carry a small kit of bandaids,
antisepectic stuff, moleskin, aspirin, and minor stuff like that
since it could be several days before I'd get into a town. Also some
really small tweezers to get those blood sucking ticks off. (Used
that a LOT in Virginia!) I figured if the injury was more serious
than that, I'd have a bunch of rescuers carrying me out anyhow and
would probably bring lots of emergency medical supplies as well. =)
And if I was in even worse shape and died, well--then medical
supplies wouldn't matter much anyhow.

I also had another theory while on the trail: A day never passed
where I didn't pass people that carried honking huge first aid kits
and if you needed a bandaid or something, there were always PLENTY of
people around that LOVED to be helpful. Weekenders and day hikers
were famous on the trail for carrying all sorts of extra unnecessary
stuff. (They tend to be more paranoid about outdoor dangers than
those that spend a lot of time out there.)

In any case, for what it's worth, I think a first aid kit is totally
unnecessary for most people, but hey, if you feel safer carrying it
around, go right ahead. Someone else that injures themselves who
doesn't have a first aid kit will welcome the fact that you brought
it! =)

As for cell phones--I tend to think they give people a false sense of
security. Most of the time, out in the mountains, they don't work to
begin with. And even when they do, you still have to know where you
are to lead rescuers to your predicament. And even if you know where
you are, if the injury is serious enough to bring in rescuers, you're
probably unconcious anyhow and can't use the phone for that reason.
You wouldn't believe how many stories I've heard about people with
cell phones calling for help because they got lost. (Usually the
same people walking around with GPS's they don't know how to use.)

No, a better idea--if you're the paranoid type, at least--is to bring
a friend that can go for help if it becomes necessary.

But then, I've been known to drink untreated water from streams and
ponds, dare bears by using my food bag as a pillow, looking for
rattlesnakes, and anything else that flies in the face of logic and
prudent outdoor skills. So what do I know? =)

-- Ryan, and haven't been killed outdoors yet!

From: Silent Doug (silentdoug@letterboxing.info) | Date: 2003-10-28 09:04:54 UTC-05:00
While I agree with Ryan that bandaids aren't going to save your life, I do
carry bandaids in my letterboxing pack -- to keep the blood of a fresh cut
from dripping onto some letterbox log!

In what I consider my "emergency" provisions, I also have some alcohol
wipes (like bandaids, they don't take up much space), a space blanket,
water purification tablets, a waterproof windbreaker or poncho, matches in
a waterproof container and a small flashlight. I always have a bottle of
water and some snack item, too.

I frequently hike alone, so my choices are based on surviving a worst-case
scenario: I slide or fall down a cliff in some remote area and am unable or
find it very difficult to walk. In that case, exposure, hypothermia and
dehydration are the biggest concerns, since it could be several days until
help arrives. I do usually have my cell phone, too, but I don't count on
being able to get a signal out in the wilderness.

BTW, those snake bite kits with the suction cups? Fuggedabut them -- they
won't do you any good. In case you're bitten by any kind of poisonous
snake, get to help ASAP -- anti-venom is the only thing that's going to
save you.

From: Twilight2000 (twilight2001@earthlink.net) | Date: 2003-10-28 08:08:35 UTC-08:00
Yes dear, but some of us hike with "smalls" -- and while i might well be able to fix an injury and call for help, i might not be able to carry her out of some of the places i've been.

as for the phone -- i check it when i'm going in -- if i'm going into an area where the reception isn't there, i simply become concious of that.

as for the med kit -- ok, confession time -- i'm a medic (or was a million years ago), so yes, i *could* set a broken leg and carry her out of *some* of the places i've been -- but not all -- hence the phone...

but even if one *isn't* a medic, bandaging (and i'm not talking a bandaid here, i'm talking serious bandaging for a wound of some sort or strapping an ankle so you *can* hike out) someone so they can make it out with help is still useful.

So what's in my med kid? ace wraps -- usually 3 or 4 -- and large squares of muslin (packaged as "slings" at most stores) -- again, usually 4 or more. Some gauze pads, some tape (either athletic or micropore -- depending on how rich i was feeling when i last purchased...). Bee sting ampules (yea, yea, yea -- but *you* go hiking with small children and let one of 'em get stung and see how much it was worth it to *you* (and the rest of the hiking group) for you to have been carrying them -- it's amazing what "calming down" the kid can do for enjoying the rest of the hike -- for both the child *and* the rest of the group). Antiseptic of various forms. And various other bits and pieces (including some "surgical" scissors (read: scissors with nubby ends so i don't cut the patient...) to cut bandages and such.

Do i carry this kit *every* time? nope -- the whole kit gets pulled out for hikes into places I don't know where I can't expect lots of traffic -- for a wander in Discovery Park in Seattle, i bring water bottles. For Rattlesnake Ridge, water bottles, unless i've got kids -- then i might bring the kit (easier to grab than take apart of the bits i really want -- that's a heavily trafficked trail). For the back side of Couger Mountain which i *don't* know, probably the whole kit.

If i was hiking solo or with other adults -- i probably *should* bring the aces and slings -- i couldn't carry most adults out anymore (we *all* weigh too much these days :>) -- but i tend to bring just water and the phone -- lazy me -- hope it doesn't bite me in the back side one day...

but that's me :)

Twilight

Good day Carie and others.

I thought I would give some of my personal suggestions, and explain
what I bring along, in accordance to this topic. I feel that carrying
something such as a first aid kit is something that should be done.
You should have one especially if you have children or are an
inexperienced hiker/letterboxer your self. What I am not saying here
is that you should carry a Case-o-Band-Aids, a case-o-Neosporin, a
case-o-Gauze and so on. We don't need to be a walking pharmacy nor
are we a Medic in a war when we are out letterboxing. So what I
carry, as pandora has mentioned, is a very small waterproof rubber
container with just a few things in it. Some Band-Aids, clean wipes,
iodine, antibacterial cream. And then I have my personal kit that I
always carry when I am by my self hiking or fishing what have you.
This includes the main item for anyone going out; a good knife,
perhaps a locking bladed knife would be your best choice, a 3-inch
wide Ace-Bandage wrap, 3-inch wide roll of gauze, some biodegradable
hiking toilet paper, Aleve, and some breakable Iodine packs.

Also a flashlight is highly recommended, I personally have a small
Mag-lite that is in my pack, and then I also I carry a compass. Most
letterboxers have a compasses, however if you are unfamiliar with
where you are at, perhaps it would be a good idea to just pull the
compass out before you trek off and take a quick reading while you
are at the car. That way in the worse of worst scenarios you at least
