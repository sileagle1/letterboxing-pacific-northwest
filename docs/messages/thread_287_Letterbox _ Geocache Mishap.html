Letterbox / Geocache Mishap

From: Mischief In Wa (Mischief_wa@hotmail.com) | Date: 2003-06-05 21:46:10 UTC
A few weeks ago I get word from Candlelight that my forth Elemental
letterbox (Water) is missing that that there is a geocache only a couple of
feet away. It wasnt till yesterday that I got a chance to investigate.
First I found the suspect geocache on the geocache website. Sure nuff,
there were some vauge comments including this post from the cache owner....

-----------------------------------
-----------------------------------
I removed the letterbox from the cache (someone had taken EVERYTHING OUT OF
THE LETTERBOX AND PUT IN THE CACHE! WRONG, WRONG, WRONG, WRONG!). I tried to
restore the letterbox, but of course someone has taken the rubber stamp from
the letterbox, so now no letterboxers can log the letterbox. Bad, bad, bad.

I put the cache back in place. I put the letterbox back in place.

I will hope that whoever took the rubber stamp from the letterbox will go
back and return it. If it isn't returned, I will contact the letterbox owner
and buy a replacement.

If there are any more people who mess up the letterbox, I will archive this
cache.

I think that would be sad but if geocachers can't resist the temptation to
spoil the letterbox I won't have any choice.

Please, please, please exercise a little judgement and commonsense. I had
hoped that it would be fun to have a letterbox and a geocache share the same
space, and have that be a surprise. Alas, the number of people who have
posted logs which spoil the surprise is disappointing. The fact that
geocachers felt free to mess up the letterbox is very disappointing. If this
sounds like I am angry it is because I am.

In comparison to the geocachers (who have stolen the stamp from the
letterbox, taken the entire contents of the letterbox and put them in the
cache, separated the letterbox note from the letterbox log book, and
generally been stupidly destructive) the letterboxers who have found the
cache apparently examined the contents, signed the log, and were careful to
replace the cache in its correct spot.

I am very, very inclined to just archive the cache and write the whole thing
off to the fact that apparently it requires more sense than people generally
have.
-----------------------------------
-----------------------------------

This pretty much wiped out any bad feelings I may have had over the issue.
He didn't mean any harm, and thought two could co-exist peacefully.

I shot off a friendly note to him, and went to check it out for myself.
Sure 'nuff no sign of the letterbox and the geocache was there (nicely
hidden). I took the time to sift thru the cache and read the log book.
It's amazing the number of geocachers there are and so few knew about
letterboxing. One person who had found the letterbox sounded confused and
suspected that it was part of "some stamping group". Heh.

I did a geocaching no-no and took something from the cache without leaving
anything behind.... But if you think about it, I did leave a whole
letterbox behind and mighty fine stamp too. I took a water-tight plastic
thingy that might make a good mini letterbox. So I call it even. ;-)

When I got home, I already had a reply from the cache owner. He was
extremely apologetic and felt very sorry that his geocache brought harm to
my letterbox. He even offered to remove the cache entirely.

So... if anyone wants the chance to see an honest to goodness geocache, go
check out my Redmond Elementals series. You'll find the cache there, where
the last letterbox would be. It's kinda cool, and there's even a log you
can stamp into if the mood strikes.

I will be replacing the letterbox (away from the geocache), but due to my
personal schedule, and the time it will take to carve a replacement, it will
take a few weeks.

~ Mischief ~

Re: [LbPNW] Re: Letterbox / Geocache Mishap

From: mudstumpers (mudstumpers@yahoo.com) | Date: 2003-06-05 16:13:34 UTC-07:00
Is there any way to be sure that we don't set up a letterbox anywhere near a geocache? From the little we know, finding a geocache where Alice in Wonderland used to be, well let's just say I don't want my boxes near a geocache. Any ideas on locating them with only a compass?

dvn2rckr wrote:This should be titled "Random thoughts on letterboxing &
geocaching"...

I'm sure our LB/geocache experiences aren't the only ones out
there--this is probably happening all over the country. Personally,
I've had a few 'interactions' with geocache(r)s in our 11 months of
letterboxing.

I've had letterboxes near geocaches go missing quite
frequently. My first one that experienced this fate was the Ft
Nisqually letterbox--one of my first letterboxes I ever placed. I
should've realized that when a geocacher left a little toy in the
box a few weeks after we placed it--at the time, we just thought it
was a modified hitchhiker and we sent it on its way (wonder where
Jims GEMS are now?). Since then, I've had about 3 other letterboxes
near geocaches go missing.

Now--when planting letterboxes (I often come across geocaches
when I'm looking for hiding spots--found about a dozen in that
manner so far) and I come across a geocache--I hide the letterbox as
far away from it as I possibly can (over 30' preferably). Inherent
GPS accuracy is not that great (for national security reasons) so
you can imagine a geocacher who is strictly following the gadget to
a given coordinate--when nearing his/her target they must just comb
the countryside to find the cache--often times, at its best the non-
military/navigational GPS can only come to within 30 feet accuracy--
from there, the geocacher must hunt and peck for the box (thus
revealing anything else they come across--typically letterboxes--in
the process).

Now, when following trails I often can tell in an instant
where a geocache is--there is typically a blazed trail (damage to
the surrounding landscape) heading to the spot. I've also tried to
get a lot better about hiding my letterboxes in spots that don't
require off trail movement so as to avoid creating these 'off-trail'
dashes. I'm also trying to camouflage my boxes as well as possible
(hence the myriad black/brown containers I'm putting out these days)
to preclude these 'accidental' discoveries from occurring.

I've met many geocachers in person (and virtually--I even spoke
with Jim whose GEMS we found last July) and they have often been
extremely nice people who share similar interests with
letterboxers. I've even met about a dozen geocachers on the trail
now, too (it's hard to miss their 'indicators' when they have a GPS
in one hand and a piece of printer paper in the other). I try to
tell them about letterboxing--if only to explain the hobby to them,
not necessarily to 'evangelize' letterboxing, so they protect any
letterboxes they stumble across in the future. I even accompanied
some of them to their geocachers and I observed little application
of/appreciation for the LNT ethic--that was enough to encourage me
to focus wholly on letterboxing (big comment coming from me because
I'm not a radical environmentalist by any definition of the word).

Occasionally, I come across geocaches while seeking letterboxes,
I typically will check in out of curiosity and try to repair it if
needed. I've also found a letterbox by hints supplied in a
geocache's log (Sunday in the Park with George series in Salem)--
never found box #2 and stumbled across the cache looking for #3.
The log had about 10 of 50 entries that discussed the nearby
letterbox. So, I figured it was still in the area. When I finally
found the letterbox, it was about 30 feet away from the cache! That
just solidified the built in inaccuracy of GPS's to me and how far
off course people get hunting for their treasures.

It certainly was great to see this gentleman's comments about
protecting the neighboring letterbox. It was quite refreshing to
read! Thanks for sharing it with us--Mischief!!!

Re: Letterbox / Geocache Mishap

From: bcostley (bobbyeubanks@yahoo.com) | Date: 2003-06-05 23:48:56 UTC
The geocache website can visually shows geocaches on a map so you
can look to see generally where they are. It's a pretty nifty
feature of the site. Also mapquest will map by coordinates. Note
that a lot of geocaches use the coordinates as a starting point and
then have clues like letterboxes. Some wind up being very far away
from the starting point which makes your life difficult if you are
trying to avoid planting near them. There are probably plenty of
geocaches you could find without ever needing a GPS. Without doing
much work probably the best you can determine is whether there is
already a geocache _likely_ in the park/area you are looking to hide
a box. Of course geocaches can show up nearby after you've hidden a
box.

Disclaimer: I've never found a geocache, this is just what I gather
from looking at the website for a while and looking at clues for
caches nearby. I know that a cache was placed near one of my boxes
after I planted it. So far without any problems that I'm aware of.
The geocache clues do mention my box and warn to make sure that
they've got the geocache and not the letterbox.

Re: Letterbox / Geocache Mishap

From: Amanda Arkebauer (samanark@yahoo.com) | Date: 2003-06-06 15:10:51 UTC
When Lea came to visit last week, she had a "travel bug" (like a HH--
except all slick and high tech) from a geocache.
Everyone is probably aware that I am a big anti-geocache person. Poor
Lea is living down in the SFO area and just ran out of letterboxes to
go after and she tried this geocaching thing. Of course the thing she
takes out of the box is the "travel bug"! The geocachers get a little
miffed with her, because someone else went to that cache specifically
looking for the bug to move it on and it was gone. Lea had
not "logged" it into the geocache website fast enough. --actually she
wasn't aware of the etiquette until she went to comment on her find
and found the comments about the "idiot who took the bug and didn't
log it in" They yelled at her a little bit, but anyway...the point of
the story is that we had to go to a geocache to return this travel
bug.

We went to Padden Puppy Trove in Bellingham, WA which is a
letterbox/geocache hybrid. In the logbook (a slick waterproof paper
book put out especially for geocaching) for the geocache portion of
this box, they consistently complained about how far off the GPS
coordinates were. Most reverted to the letterbox clues to find the
thing. It was a honking big ammo box with a slick Geocache sticker on
the side. The whole commercial/corporate sponsership of the thing
makes my skin crawl. How expensive must it be for these folks to buy
the waterproof books and the stickers and these ammo boxes and then
all the little toys and dodads???!!! I have been over to the
geocaching site and it is very slick and high tech. AND meant to sell
GPS's and the assorted paraphernalia. I can see how it can suck in
people. Especially guys (no offense) who like that sort of high tech
gadgetry. And little kids who like little crappy dollar store toys.
(again no offense)

--Amanda from Seattle
