From: dvn2rckr (dvn2rckr@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-01-16 04:37:39 UTC
Salmon are found in many different locations beyond the Northwest--
so it isn't as indicative of the region as some other things are.
(I harp on the orca simply because it's the 'Pacific' group--the
only other US regions that could use that image would really be
Alaska and possibly coastal California if they're lucky enough to
see them during migration--Puget Sound has the resident orca
population--of course, BC Canada could run with the orca since
they've got most of em visible from land!!!).

For a stamp or a logo for that matter, less is usually more--it's
easier on the eye. More intricacy usually creates problems. Love
the Native designs, though--there are some that don't have as much
intricacy but still incorporate that 'native culture' feel to them.
One route to incorporate many things into one patch is to use a
primary image--salmon, orca, sun and use the inner design to
incorporate different regional elements (mountains, animals, hiking
boot, tree, banana slug, etc).

Many of the other regional patches incorporate the similar LB
font/colors--which tie them into the parent entity. Deviating from
that segregates the regional group from the parent. If you spell
out the lettering, what about using the original LBNA font/colors
for the letters that correspond to the parent. Also, the little 'A'
arrow-like image I think is an orienteering graphic (not certain)
but I have a feeling it's tied to that hobby in some way--perhaps
it's simply an arrow pointing north? I presume Randy Hall would
know--or whoever designed the LBNA graphic (might be DMS?). Who
knows? I figure there's a specific reason why LBNA used that to
begin with.

East & West sides of the Cascades from both states share the
mountains--so perhaps a mountain with green/tropical on the left
(West) base and brown/arid on the (East) right base would
incorporate both?

Just some things to think about.

The ideas so far are really cool!

dvn2r ckr
