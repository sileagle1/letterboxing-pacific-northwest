Distance between Letterboxes

From: funhog1 (funhog@pacifier.com) | Date: 2004-06-24 18:42:49 UTC
Now here's a topic ripe for discussion: How close to another letterbox should a new
box be placed?

In a recent email, a finder of my Oaks Bottom box discovered another letterbox
literally on top of mine. In fact, he nearly missed mine because it was shoved so deep.
I know that there are no rules regarding such placing but I find it annoying. I take a
lot of time ferreting out good spots. This hidey hole is not big enough for two boxes
to be well hidden.

I have hidden boxes in the same park, on the same mountain or on the same trail as
other boxes but never anywhere near them. When I make a series, I try to have the
boxes at least a ten minute walk apart. That way there is less potential for
environmental damage and the development of social trails. Those social trails are
the very thing that gets geocaches removed from parks and I don't want that to be
the case with my letterboxes.

When boxes are really close together, it also requires that you hang around an area
much longer stamping in. This would seem to lead to a greater chance of discovery
by and onlooker and the possible loss of letterboxes. I also like to have time between
boxes to warm up a bit if I'm hiking in the winter.

These are a few of my thoughts... anyone else??? Funhog

P.S. Incidentally, anyone know who owns that box on top of mine at Oaks Bottom?
apparently there is no clue to it and no contact info inside...

From: pandora{I`B} (hispandora@phlsystems.com) | Date: 2004-06-30 23:53:15 UTC-07:00
I'm a little behind in my response to this thread so please forgive my delay
- Iron`Bear and I have been doing a lot of boxing lately.



I too happened across the Oaks Bottom box with the "Big Y" box planted
practically on top of it - and I also do not think there is any possible way
that it could have been a hitch hiker - it was simply too large, and it
contained an ink pad and a pen - not something you typically see in a hitch
hiker.



Also throwing my two cents in - and I realize that's not worth more than two
pennies - so take it for what it's worth. I welcome many things in my boxes,
hitch hikers, coo coo clues. (I was so excited to see Miller and Hutch had
left their clues in one of my boxes). However I would prefer boxes were not
planted either on top of mine or too close to mine. I value discretion when
finding my boxes, often times when I find a letterbox I will even walk away
from the location I found it in and stamp in else where to avoid being seen
near the location. (Unless the trail is simply not often traveled.) I would
hope that many people would do the same thing with my boxes. And personally
I really would not want another box within 30-40 feet of mine. I understand
that sometimes people plant boxes without realizing there is another box
there, but I think some extra time researching your location when planting
would help alleviate that. I know that I go to extra effort to make sure I
don't use the same name for a letterbox that someone else locally has
already used, and I definitely don't plant boxes in the same exact area. I
recently planted a series up at the Audubon Sanctuary very near Funhog's
"What's For Dinner", But I did it at the recommendation of Funhog herself
when I had told her that I didn't have a home for this wonderful new
hummingbird series I had. In addition to getting her permission we took very
careful measure to make sure we were far enough away from any of her boxes.



I think the other thing to keep in mind as a letter boxer is that often
times we all plant things in places that are very special to us for one
reason or another, My Love Shack Wedding Memorial box for example, was
planted in the first Park Iron`Bear and I ever went to together. While
there were other boxes planted there, I was careful to go in a different
direction from them entirely. If we keep in mind that perhaps the places
other letter boxers are taking us to are very special and endearing to them,
we might understand why they would get upset about something like the
environment being trampled on around their boxes, or another letterbox being
too nearby.



In my opinion if you know that there is another letterbox near by, or even
on the same trail for that matter a common courtesy to the placer might be
to contact them via email first and ask them if they mind if you plant one
in a nearby location.



I understand totally that everyone has as much a right to plant a box as I
do, anywhere they want, but I personally think that we all try to be
respectful of each other's feelings and of each other's boxes, and I think
that is what makes this pacific northwest group such a wonderful group of
people.



When I was in California I ran into a situation where someone used my
letterbox as a clue for theirs. I have seen lots of people put at the end
of their clues things like "While in the area you might want to check out
pandora's Turtle Soup Letterbox" and I think that is wonderful, let people
know if something else is within their range, but I didn't appreciate my
letterbox being used as a starting point without having being asked first.
Plus, what if my box goes missing? I just think anytime you are going to do
anything that has to do with another letterbox, its clues, or it's location
you should have the permission of the creator of that letterbox.



One other thing to remember is that any area that has people poking around
and prodding in several different area's is liable to draw attention, so by
planting a letterbox to close to another I think we jeopardize compromising
all boxes in that area when people start wondering what we were poking
around for. I recently planted a series geared for children where all the
letterboxes are within about 30ft of each other and even doing that
purposely I was concerned about how much attention I would draw to that
location. But it was an experiment - so we'll see what happens.



Again, just my two cents worth -

pandora{I`B}
