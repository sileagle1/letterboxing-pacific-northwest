When the Washington Post article on letterboxing came out way back
when
we had just visited Chantilly, Virginia for a several week business
trip and hiked in the very park they mentioned in the article. I
couldn't believe that there were little 'boxes' hidden along the
very
trails we'd hiked that we hadn't even noticed. East Coast 'woods'
are
quite different from the West Coast in that the trees seem to be
very 'sparse' and there's little to no 'underbrush' so you can see
for
yards and yards unlike here in the NW. So, that's what initially
intrigued me--how could they possibly hide a letterbox in that
terrain
that yielded few to no natural hiding spots? At the time, I was a
frustrated injured athlete who was desperate for a form of exercise
that would not exacerbate my injuries---letterboxing was the perfect
way to go. Easy on the body, allowed you to go at your own pace
and 'bonus!' you didn't need a babysitter for the kids!

Since then the clue writing, discovering new spots and the carving
has
grown on me. Definitely the gift of 'expression' in the clue
writing
and interweaving bits of history resulting from my research of
unique things
continue to pique my interest, too!

dvn2r ckr

From: Scott Carpenter (Scott@StuftDzine.com) | Date: 2005-12-12 12:12:26 UTC-08:00
I'm new (about three weeks into the hobby) and only have found 10 or 11 so
far. We discovered letterboxing accidentally when googling Elk Rock Island
on an afternoon outing. I had just finished reading Fugitives and Refugees:
A Walk in Portland, Oregon by Chuck Palahniuk (highly recommended for all
visitors or residents of PDX - or for any homosapien who likes a good travel
journal - not really a guide book but a series of observations from his 25
years living in PDX) and wanted to check out Elk Rock Island since I live
real close by and had never even heard of it. Well, the first thing that
popped up was Der Mad Stampers Elk Rock Island box so since two 4 year olds
were going on the trip I figured . . . treasure hunt - GREAT!!! Well we
figured out the part about the tree but spent most of the afternoon trying
to figure out what the #$%^ green 13 was (I figured it out last weekend from
across the river - haven't gone back yet though) and after the girls began
to melt we abandoned the effort, but I was hooked and spent most of the
evening reading about letterboxing on the web. The fact that there were 6
boxes listed within walking distance of my house (Sellwood area) got me out
the next few days to collect Adele, Sophie, Wallace Books, & Oaks Bottom.
Looked for Quackers . . . couldn't find it (but found a geocahce and now
have $%^&*!@ Christmas colorforms all over my windows) and looked quickly
for the Oaks Amusement one (listed as MIA on the web).

Anyhow, that's my story.

From: funhog1 (funhog@pacifier.com) | Date: 2005-12-17 19:10:50 UTC
It would seem my neighbor knew more about me than I knew myself. When
he was taught about letterboxing while getting his Master of Arts in
Teaching degree at Lewis and Clark College, he copied all the
classroom materials and handed them to me. He figured I just might be
interested. Little did he know what a monster he'd released!

I have long been an enthusiastic hiker; I travel all over the dang
place for business; and I am very curious about solving mysteries. All
of these fit perfectly with the wacky hobby of letterboxing. Once I
studied up, I also realized that the underground aspects of boxing
made it all the more appealing. I love feeling that I'm doing
something sneaky but am most likely not going to get into any kind of
trouble for it. It feeds that rebellious kid side of my adult self.

BTW, the neighbor who first introduced me to this rolls his eyes and
laughs when I tell him about my latest adventures. He now teaches
fifth grade in SE Portland. I guess I was one of his first students.
Funhog
