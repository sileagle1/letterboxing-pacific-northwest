What is so ironic is that when I hid #1 I was just stewing about it
all day and that night and for days to follow that it was in a poor
spot and would probably go missing because of passers-by stumbling
onto it or dogs sniffing it out - it's hard to find good spots in such
a highly-used area. I even went up there a few days later to relocate
it, stood around for about 15 minutes to assess the traffic, and
changed my mind thinking it might be alright after all. I considered
it the easiest of the 3 to find and the clues the most direct and
"easy" - a very good example of what people often think when they are
writing clues, that they are hard or easy or whatever, and then
everyone else finds them just the opposite. One of the things I find
really fascinating about letterboxing, because I'm especially
interested in words and word usage and language in general - it's
basically what I do for a living. I'll be looking for a box and think
to myself "well just what the *(#%$ to they mean by "stump" or "big
fir tree" or some other term, when I'm sure it was obvious to the
clue-writer. I have a box at Falls Creek, for which I thought the
clue was cutesy but easy and obvious (one of my rare clues of FEW
words), and yet only one person has been able to find it. I guess the
old saying about beauty is true even applied to letterboxing clues -
"it's in the eye of the beholder."
