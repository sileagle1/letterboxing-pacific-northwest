From: Nail Family (c.nail@comcast.net) | Date: 2003-09-30 03:00:35 UTC
I just recieved as a birthday gift a nifty little handheld GPS unit.
Up until the last year, the only reason I would have ever wanted to
have one would be for fishing, hiking, and general navigation
purposes. I am a tried and true letterboxer but geocaching does
appeal to me too. I have stumbled on 2 of them and have followed
clues to a letterbox that was hidden very close to another. The
trinkets are not interesting to me (although my kids really like the
idea of trading junk) but the idea of the treasure hunt is, almost
as much as letterboxing. I may even place some caches myself.
However, I think that if I do, I will make them more like
letterboxes- mystery clues that you have to solve, perhaps by
visitng other sites in the general area of the cach in order to get
the information to fill in the correct coordinates. That is much
more challenging in my opinion than just following a pointer on a
handheld. I suppose that if I had had a GPS unit in my possession
when I first heard of letterboxing and geocaching, I would have
chosen geocaching over letterboxing---in the beginning that is. I
still think that letterboxing is more appropriate for kids. The
compass navigation, tree and plant identification, cryptogram
solving, and other research needed to solve clues is much more
applicable to education than just following a pointer. The gps in my
opinion is just another tool to be mastered in an overall arsenal of
tools at our disposal especially in relation to educating my kids.

Another appealing thing about 'caching is that there are many of
them nearby--I dont have to travel to another county to get my
treasure hunting fix. I mean, how many boxes can dvn2rckr place in
the Olympia area anyway? I can think of only 2 parks she or myself
or fudrick hasnt placed in yet.

Taking advantage of all adventures at my disposal,
Nailhead
