I agree with Funhog that we should "stick to the existing trails." But I'd
argue that the Red Fox box is not on a trail established by the park. It's on
a "user trail"--one created by park users, taking shortcuts or having a look
at what's over there. Usually these trails are discouraged by park
personnel--they often place logs/tree limbs across such paths in an attempt to discourage
people from continuing to use them. It bothered me to go "off trail" after
that box--but I have to confess that I did it anyway.

It's been some months since I've been there, but I'd bet that trail is still
passable. It was pretty well established. That box gave Heather & me a tough
time ("an upright log" is the phrase that I think is used--threw us for a
loop--what the heck? Not a tree, but an upright log??? Of course, when we found
it we realized the description is accurate.) I bet the kids just gave up too
soon. And I echo Mudstumpers' comment that the tree mentioned in the clues
has fallen.

From: Nail Family (c.nail@comcast.net) | Date: 2003-10-07 06:01:45 UTC
The Nail Family went out on their very first geocaching excursion
yesterday and we were amazed at how easily an area can get trampled.
We found the cache in question not even near the tramplings. I
guess it had to do with poor satelite reception around trees and
hills. I am quite intrigued by gecocaching (almost as much as
letterboxing) but if I do place any caches myself I will post
coordinates to a very unmistakable location and make very direct
clues as to the final hiding spot. I think that there are many
great adventure posibilities in caching but that there needs to be
more creative effort in hiding and giving the clue/coordinates in
order to minimize the possibility of bushwacking. Maybe our year of
letterboxing experience can aid in changing the tide of detrimental
caching.

PS. dont worry, we arent giving up caching, we just got a new toy
and want to take advantage of the many adventures near our home
since we dont get to travel too much and we have found almost all
the 'boxes in our home area. We will continue to create letterboxes
and strive to make them interesting, challenging, fun and exciting.
Maybe by participating in both sports we can even help to convert
some of the bushwackers into respectable treasure hunters too.
