From: rscarpen (letterboxing@atlasquest.com) | Date: 2005-10-06 03:32:32 UTC
Besides the horrible shock of walking down the street and learning about this from the
large, front page title showing out of a sidwalk kiosk, I got to thinking about it.

And you know, I don't think it's really as big of a tragidy as my knee-jerk reaction thought
it was. Think about it. The fort was only a replica in the first place built in 1955. So the
current fort is hardly 'historic' per se. Not like the *original* fort would have been if it was
still around.

And it's a relatively small, cheap log cabin kind of thing. A couple of dozen men threw it
up in 1805 in three weeks with tools only found from the early 1800's! Seems that,
relatively speaking, it's not a multi-million dollar building that went up in flames. It's all
replaceable!

And now that it burned and the plan to rebuild, they can do an even more accurate job of
recreating the fort. They've learned a lot of details about the original fort that they didn't
know about in 1955 and a recreated structure could be even more historically accurate
than the one that went up in flames.

All-in-all, it might be inconvient for school children and tourists for a bit of time, but
nothing seems to have been lost that isn't easily replaceable, and the new fort could be an
improvement!

Silver linings are always there... =)

On another note, in the news article I read, it said they didn't know what started the fire
yet. I'm not an expert on fires or anything, but I'll go out on a limb and suggest it probably
wasn't due to faulty wiring. ;o)

-- Ryan
