Thank you Camp Fire Lady and Cat's Meow --

I'm glad you guys made it up there - I have to agree with you that the Trail
Where They Cried is quite the treacherous trail; I think we had Ryan and
Funhog in mind when we planted that series - we wanted to give them
something a little harder than our normal boxes to do. I think the other
influence was that I have to agree with you - the stamp images for the Trail
where they cried is by far the best carving I have ever done and really
shows Iron Bear's artistic ability - we had found a few Indian images that
were what he was looking for - but when he freehand drew them and altered
them to fit his location I was amazed at what wonderful stamp images they
made. Also, the letterboxes where planed before we got there, it was an
area Iron`Bear had been to before and had some very special experiences at,
at each stamp was very specifically drawn to where it was located. So when
we discovered that there had been a fire there, we decided to plant them
anyway, I think the name of the series is very fitting to the trail and the
story involved in the letterbox is something we can all learn a little bit
of Native American history from.

I'm so happy you were able to get the images, and I totally agree Cat's Meow
deserves a pat on the back - Iron`Bear and I would not have taken Sim Kitty
on that trial, so I think Cat's Meow did magnificent.

And having seen you both when you stopped by after your hiking and can
honestly say it was the first time you looked more tired than she did!
Hehehe

Thank you again for all the complements,
Pandora{I`B} (I'm sure Iron`Bear would agree, but he's still sleeping)

Last Saturday, Cat's Meow and had took an afternoon drive out to
Detroit, Oregon for some awesome letterboxes, The Trail Where They
Cried, The Black Dahlia, Yellow Rose of Texas, My First Fish by
Pandora and Iron Bear and Mudstumpers' Ollalie Lake. All are alive
and well and some of the most beautiful stamps I have ever seen! The
Trail Where They Cried was the most challenging and beautiful hike we
have ever done and I was so proud of Cat's Meow for being able to
finish the hike with me. The story behind the clues and the scenery
was so inspiring that you had to attempt it but the trail that you
need to follow at times would disappear and become so treacherous
that it was difficult to navigate. Walking through the burned out
forest was a very sad experience but beautiful at the same time
because you are passing through a lush green forest into a barren
section of land but coming upon 3 different lakes to cool off in. The
stamp images are well worth the blood, sweat and tears that you will
shed along the way. These are stamps that I will treasure forever and
an experience that I will never forget! Thank you Pandora and Iron
Bear for creating such a wonderful series!
Camp Fire Lady and Cat's Meow

Let us know if you plan on joining us then.

Perhaps we'll see you there?

Subject: WA: King County New Letterbox Ah, Little Mt. Si!

New letterbox: Ah, Little Mt. Si!
North Bend, King County, WA
Placed by: Danny
Box webpage located at:

Clues:
Ah, Little Mt. Si! Letterbox
Planted on July 25, 2004 by Keegan the Airedale Terrier, Piper, Toast Boy
and Loud Eyes.
Day Hikers Only
No Bicycles or Horses
This is a 5-mile roundtrip hike.
1,200 feet gained in elevation.
The top of Little Mt. Si (pronounced sigh) is 1,576 feet above sea level.
The trail has some steep sections with roots and rocks that require hand
holds. Hiking time roundtrip is approximately 3 to 4 hours. Be sure to take
the 10 essentials plus water and/or your favorite beverage. A camera and
binoculars will come in handy. Colored markers will make the rubber stamped
image cooler!

Driving directions:

Drive I-90 to the town of North Bend and take exit 31 into town. Turn right
heading east on the main drag of North Bend Way. Just east of town turn
left, heading north on Mount Si Road (432nd SE). Cross the bridge over the
Middle Fork of the Snoqualmie River. Bear right just past the bridge and
drive a 1/4 mile more to the new parking lot on the left. The lot is
designed to hold 50 cars and includes a new outhouse. If the lot is full,
you can park back at the old lot next to the bridge and walk to the
trailhead.

This is the first letterbox planted in an eventual series along the trail
to the summit of Little Mt. Si. Clues will be added to this webpage, once
other letterboxes are in place. Please stay tuned!

>From the parking lot, follow the signs to the top of Little Mt. Si. The
>trail snakes through a beautiful forest and past rock faces used by rock
>climbers.

Once on top, take in the nearly 180 degree view of the Snoqualmie River
Valley, Snoqualmie Pass, McClellan Butte, Mailbox Peak, Rattle Snake Ledge,
North Bend, and of course Mt. Si.

Clues:

Embedded in the rock at the top of Little Si are two metal, round National
Geodetic Survey Reference Markers. The northern most marker is at the
highest point of Little Si. Search for the second marker approximately 20
paces towards the south. Stand on top of the southern lower marker and take
a compass bearing of 250 degrees magnetic. Scramble down hill in this
direction for approximately 20 to 25 paces. Look for a blown down tree with
a root ball. Inside the protected root ball is a rock cairn that contains
the Ah, Little Mt. Si! Letterbox.


This is my seventh planted letterbox. Please email me with any questions or
comments at Keegan62002@yahoo.com.
