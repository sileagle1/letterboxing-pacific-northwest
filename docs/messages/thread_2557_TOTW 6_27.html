From: Jeanne Ginter (heronafoot@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-06-27 19:57:51 UTC
"What have you lost while letterboxing?"

On our way to the Spring Flinger I lost my signature stamp. On the
north Oregon coast I stamped into a box in the parking/ visitor's
center. Then we went on a beautiful less than a mile hike to another
box. When I sat down on the moss to stamp into the second box my
signature stamp was nowhere to be found.

"What have you lost while letterboxing?"

We walked the trail back and forth 3 times looking - asked the staff
at the visitors center and phoned to the main office leaving a message
of how to contact me if it was turned in to any lost & found. Never
saw it again.

"This could be a fun question."

Now the Rest of the Story" I am on my way to my very first gathering
and have I no signature stamp. We stopped in Astoria, OR (the largest
city on our route)to look for carving medium and tools. Was sent 4
places before a lady in a cute little paper shop offered to sell me
some from her private craft box. That nite I sat in our motel room at
the little round table under the 40 watt light trying to reproduce my
signature stamp. The results were far from satisfactory but at least
I had something to use for exchanges and boxes and a funny memory.

H-A-F

From: funhog1 (funhog@pacifier.com) | Date: 2006-06-27 22:29:51 UTC
Well, this one was a doozey. When Doubltree showed up on the local
scene and started hiding boxes on loooong hikes last summer, I was
beside myself with excitement. I rounded up Der Mad Stamper and we
headed out the find this "newbie's" remote offerings at Ruckel Creek.
The clue assured us that we would have a hard two and a half mile hike
to the farthest box, but no more. After covering about four miles and
having never found the landmarks noted in the clue DMS and I cried
"Uncle!" turned our sweat-drenched, svelte little bodies around to
trudged back down the dreadful grade.

Starting downhill, I realized that I needed to trim my big toenails or
risk the dreaded black-falling-off toenail syndrome. Being the
well-prepared hiker that I am, I always carry nail clippers in my
backpack. I unloaded my pack in the middle of the trail and found no
clippers. Having changed packs that very morning, they were resting
peacefully in the old pack at home. Oh well, I just threw caution to
the wind, packed everything back up and hit the trail. A little over
an hour later, we stopped to have a rest and a nibble.

The juicy orange slices and delicious German chocolate cookies (love
that Trader Joe's) were calling my name. I opened my pack to get them
out when I realized that the cool little insulated sack they were in
was no longer in my possession. I had left it on the trail, miles
back. Then I realized that my prescription sunglasses were in that
bag, too. Oh, no! What to do?

DMS, gentleman that he is, offered to go back with me if I decided
that it had to be done. We looked at our water bottles: almost empty.
We looked at our watches: almost sunset. We looked at ourselves:
wobbly, dilapidated messes. We couldn't possibly add another three
miles to our day. So we proceeded back down to the trailhead, leaving
the snacks and glasses for the bear whose scat we'd seen on the trail
earlier in the day. Just for drill I left my name and phone number
with the camp host at the parking lot. We hadn't seen but two other
hikers the entire day and the likelihood of someone finding my
precious stuff before the bear did was nil... or so I thought. By the
time I'd driven the forty miles back to my house, my little black bag
was safely in the hands of the host. A distinctly non-ursine trekker
had found it, eaten all the treats and turned it in.

BTW one toenail went black but didn't fall off, just got lumpy. The
hike is hard but wonderful, especially the thousand year old vision
quest pits you see near the first box. Doubletree's stamps are so nice
that we did the same killer hike the very next week for that pesky,
elusive box that was just ten minutes farther up the trail from our
first "I give up" point. The clue has since been amended and the
correct distance noted. Ruckel Creek comes highly recommended. Just be
sure to trim your toenails before you go.

Well, we have left our sig stamps out on the trail more than once. When we
were in Connecticut, we were doing several boxes on the same trail, so we
noticed by the next box that we were missing something important! A quick
(or not so quick) trot back to the previous location to scoop up our stamps
laying on the ground in their baggie, then onward again... Unbelievably I
think this happened at least twice.

... and then we also left them at a box in a cemetery in our own town. It
wasn't near a grave, but instead on a rock ledge, and since it was the only
box we were doing that day we didn't realize we had forgotten them. Luckily
other LBers were at the same box later on the same day and recognized our
stamps. They notified us via email before we even knew they were missing.
That means we luckily didn't have to deal with panic and rushing back to a
cemetery. We received them in the mail before our next outing the following
weekend.

We also lost a compass in Pennsylvania, which meant we had to find a store
that sold them. Luckily it was in a fairly populated area and it only took
us two stops to be successful. We now always carry TWO compasses, one in
each of our packs and also keep one in our car.

Wisconsin Hiker

From: Kris Leibrand (bookwormlb@comcast.net) | Date: 2006-07-03 23:41:26 UTC
Re: TOTW 6/27

"What have you lost while letterboxing?"


Back in March when we were having all that snow and very cold
weather here in Portland, I went looking for a mystery box with
Maiden1974 and her kids after I got off of work. We were poking
around some rocks and had just found the box when my car keys fell
out of my pocket. I thought I knew right where they had fallen in
the rocks and determined I would get them after I stamped in and was
putting the box back. When I put the box back, I couldn't find them
anywhere. They weren't where I thought they were. I looked around
all the rocks nearby. Nothing. Maiden came along and helped me
move some impressively big boulders and still no keys. We poked
around with my hiking stick only to come up empty handed. We
finally determined they had fallen down this really deep crevass in
between some rocks and into the creek. I kissed my keys goodbye
right then and there.

But that's not the end of the story. I had to call my husband to
come and bring me the extra set of keys. He's not too hip on
letterboxing, and I hadn't told him I was stopping for this one, so
not only did I have to confess that I'd been letterboxing, I had to
ask for help. He was home sick with a migraine, but he drug himself
out and drove 40 miles (in the snow, at rush hour, on a Friday) to
come and rescue me. Luckily, my husband is a very smart man and
thought to stop and buy a long magnet on a rope and was able to fish
my keys out of the long, dark hole they had fallen in.
Surprisingly, a couple of hours in the near freezing water didn't
hurt my electronic door lock opener. In fact it works better now!

I was grounded from letterboxing for two weeks. :-)
Bookworm
