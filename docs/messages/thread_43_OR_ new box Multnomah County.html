I’ll get to the clues in a minute, but first a brief rant about the value of
independent bookstores, like Wallace Books: One of the many reasons not to
buy your books from a chain bookstore (Barnes & Noble, Waldenbooks, etc.) is
that publishers pay the chains to promote their books. This means that a
great book by a little publisher can languish on a bottom shelf somewhere in
a chain store (if they include it in their inventory at all), while a lousy
book gets top shelf or center-table billing because it was published by a big
house able to buy that promotion.

Independent booksellers, on the other hand, promote the books they love and
think that you might love, too. Supporting independents = a greater variety
of books and a greater diversity of authors.

So look up the answers to the clues for this box on a chain’s website if you
want—but buy independent! Okay *hopping down from my soapbox* here’s what
you need to know to find the box:

Some books (most?) become bestsellers BECAUSE of the paid promotion they
receive. It's true that they'd better live up to the hype, or the author's
next book is unlikely to sell as well. But meanwhile, a little gem can be
ignored, and die an ignoble death ("remainders") because its publisher can't
afford the publicity it deserves. IF enough critics applaud it, people may
seek it out in the store--and if enough people do that, then sure, the chain
will happily jump on that bandwagon.

But a person who's just browsing is likely to be drawn to those nice,
paid-for displays.

Barbara Kingsolver, to name one author, says she owes her success to
independent booksellers. They were the ones who promoted her first
books--the owners read 'em and recommended them to their patrons--the chains
didn't promote them at first.

Unfortunately, in this time of publishing-house mergers, fewer and fewer
large publishers are willing to take chances on quirky books, or books that
interest a (relatively) small audience.

So buy independent, Ryan!

--Wild Woman
