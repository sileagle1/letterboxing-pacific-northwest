Hi there Kathy --

Der Mad Stamper actually was from around here - he was one of the first
Pioneers of letterboxing in this area. For unknown reasons he has sort of
dropped out of the letterboxing community however and is not contactable,
reachable or active in this area any longer. Hence the reason people are
adopting his boxes.

The adopting boxes thing is a rather new thing as Green Hornet was saying
earlier and to be quite honest, is something I am not sure I totally agree
with. Adopting his boxes in the Tualatin District was a necessity per the
instructions of Kyle Spinks who works for THPRD however outside of that, I
know I personally do not want my boxes adopted. I have several boxes in CA
that were planted there before I moved. They have recently come up missing
and or incomplete and quite honestly, I will mark the boxes as retired, and
those who got them the year they were there got them, those that didn't well
sorry that is part of the game.

Some people feel differently I am sure, some people mark their boxes as
orphans and ask for others to adopt them, I've seen Bethany and Don and Gwen
do it -- I think in that case it's totally cool - if the placer is
contactable and asking for assistance and you want to help right on. However
in this case the boxes in questions can get no permissions from the original
placer and hold a significant amount of history to the letterboxing
community, which gets very passionate about things at times, and personally
outside of the THPRD area, I would suggest leaving the boxes alone, in an
effort to not piss anyone off. When this originally came up I thought it was
awesome that people wanted to preserve history, but seeing the uproar it
caused and how upset Lily got, perhaps it's just not a good idea. But that
again is just my opinion, everyone else has to make up their own mind.

That's just my two cents,
Pandora

Gosh, I'm not sure I can do an adequate introduction but I'll give it a try. When
letterboxing first started in the US, there was a small group of people whose interest
was piqued by an article in Smithsonian magazine, April '98. After months of poking
around on the internet, a half dozen or so found each other.

Luckily for us in the PNW two of these people lived here in Portland, Thom Cheney
(Green Hornet) and Mitch Klink (Der Mad Stamper.) Green Hornet hid Oregon's first
box and probably the first on the West Coast. DMS is a wonderful carver and was a
prolific letterbox hider in the early days. He hid the first boxes in many other states
as well.

Early on, they were the only two letterboxers in the area. They hid box after box for
each other to find. Some of these gems are still out there! Mitch was also the person
responsible for the original LbNA web site and it's his old timey graphics that you see
whenever you go to that site today. In recent years, the site has undergone some
wonderful updates but DMS's tutorials are still there in all their inspirational glory.

Unfortunately, Mitch has faded from the local letterboxing scene. Thom is still around
but has a very full plate, so he hasn't hidden many boxes of late. However, he's
lurking as we speak and I hope that we see some of his rad-cool stamps in another
box soon. Hear me, Green Hornet? Get out there and hide 'em!

So that's my version of a quickie history lesson. Anyone else, feel free to add
on...Funhog
