Of the 200+ letterboxes we've planted, I have two that are store
bought. These were 'intentional' and a several dozen letterbox
plants into our letterboxing adventure--just so I can say I have
some store bought ones out there. I, too, prefer handmade over
storebought stamps but usually don't make that the only 'determining
factor' on whether we seek out a particular box. We usually seek
out a box based on location or historical value to the clues. In
fact, most of the time I don't even know what type of stamp is in
any letterbox we're seeking. I also have a handful of planted
letterbox stamps that are school erasers carved with either the fine
point of thread scissors or the empty metal 'base' to a pencil
eraser pinched to create a gouge (used in desperation while planting
a letterbox on a camping trip far away from home back in July
2002). These stamps are downright HIDEOUS!!! It's up to letterbox
seekers to figure out which letterboxes contain these 'crusty'
examples of my lack of artistic ability. My hideous stamps just
serve as a great reminder to me that ANYBODY, even folks without an
artistic bone in their body (i.e. ME), can learn how to carve
stamps!!!

From: Der Mad Stamper (letterboxr@aol.com) | Date: 2005-07-19 08:09:23 UTC
Just my two cents worth, so take it with a grain of salt... If
someone has already planted a box with a store-bought stamp in it, I
would tell them that's better than not planting a box at all.
However, if they have since learned to carve fairly well, I would
encourage them to replace the stamp next time they visit that box...
or have someone replace it for them.

As much as I appreciate the effort that goes into carving a stamp by
hand, I have no problem at all with customized factory-commissioned
stamps. To me, the point is that the only way possible to get a copy
of the stamp image is to visit the letterbox.

Of course, as you may suspect, I am a fanatical rubber stamp
collector. Therefore, I have thousands and thousands of rubber
stamps. So, if you use a stamp that came off of a shelf or from a
catalog, there is a good chance that I already have that very same
stamp sitting at home in my collection. Finding such a box might make
me feel good about the size of my collection, but it bores me to
tears when I stamp it into my letterboxing journal. I prefer stamps
that are one-of-a-kind.

Now there are a few people out there who will try to tell you that
some handcarved stamps are not worthy of being in a letterbox. But
not me! I'm definitely not an artistic snob. Any old stamp will
do... just so there's not another one like it anywhere in the world.
To be honest, I have found a few that looked like someone ran an
eraser through a tree shredder. I display them in my logbook with
pride because I know they are completely unique, and they are medals
of honor to commemorate my efforts in finding the boxes they were in.

But I've been guilty, too. I have had boxes go missing, and was
short on time, so I replaced a couple of them with store-bought
stamps. But I ended up feeling like I owed the people who found them
an apology. In retrospect, I think I would have rather posted a note
in the clues indicating that the box was missing... at least until
such time that I was able to replace it with a hand-carved one. I
have since replaced such stamps with hand-carved ones.

Please don't ever feel that you need to say you're sorry for placing
a letterbox with a store-bought stamp. But also try to understand
that people are going to great lengths to find your box, and they
deserve something special and UNIQUE as a reward for their efforts.

Der Mad Stamper
