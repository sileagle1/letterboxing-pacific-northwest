From: dvn2rckr (dvn2rckr@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-03-25 15:30:39 UTC
Haven't found any 'inexpensive' sources for small, unlined notepads--
unless you make them yourself. I've used logbooks that run the
gamut from cutting small, memo notepads in half or thirds; cutting
thin long strips of heavier weight printer paper, stacking them,
folding in half and stapling the top (usually the ones I put in the
film canister LBs), unlined index cards cut in half/thirds--holes
punched in top and looped together with a metal 'binder clip' ring
or tied together with some sort of fastener, etc.

Some artistic folks (a group of which I am NOT a member) make their
own absolutely gorgeous logbooks--finding 'how to' instructions at
various websites on the 'net--using card stock, bookbinding thread,
craft papers, etc. I totally lack those skills/patience so haven't
even tried the 'professional' looking logbooks. Also, IKEA has some
really neat tiny logbooks these days--I think they run about 4-6 in
a pack for about $1--what I'm not sure is if they're unlined or
not. I think they may be lined, though.

I really haven't worried about the 'unlined' vs ruled issue in boxes
I placed (I presume because they go missing so often) except when it
comes to our personal logbook we carry on our various treks. I
learned the hard way after using a ruled journal style book as our
first logbook--not only was it ruled but I stamped on both sides of
the pages--something, upon reflection, I wish I hadn't done.

Glad to see your new boxes in the area!

Thank you for your kind comments. I guess Lemony Snicket & I have a
LOT in common. ;) Although, I prefer to maintain a more positive
outlook on life than does our narrator friend! Yes, I've never been
too keen on putting much effort into my logbooks--the one or two
times I really went above & beyond my standard logbooks, the
letterboxes either went missing right away (Yashiro Pagoda LB--
multiple times in its case) or nobody ever bothered to seek out that
particular letterbox (Wright LB) during its 'lifetime'. Personally,
I prefer to spend more mental energy and physical effort on the
stamp and especially moreso researching, entwining history with
hints aka 'wordcrafting' the clues.

DEEP THOUGHTS ON CLUE WRITING (not by jack handey)?: However, most,
if any, feedback I get to the clues I write/letterboxes we place
tends to lean toward the negative, so I wonder if writing more
straightforward clues is what most people prefer (or if people are
just more inclined to comment on letterboxes when they feel
something is 'out of whack' with the clues/trail/indicators/LB,
etc)? But, alas, don't hold your breath waiting for easier clues to
appear for most of our boxes--I don't plan on going that route. The
mystery, challenge & exploring something new is what most appeals to
me about letterboxing in the first place!!! I do plan, however, to
modify many of our existing boxes' clues to improve/tweak (not
simplify) them someday soon.

Amidst our current 'instant gratification type of a culture', I tend
to like to challenge folks' thought processes with the clues, giving
children & adults alike an opportunity to think (sometimes outside
the box), solve simple puzzles, and possibly learn some local
history or gain a greater appreciation for something they otherwise
would never explore in the process of seeking out a particular
letterbox we place. Hopefully, that's what we've accomplished with
many of our letterboxes out and about.

LOGBOOK IDEAS:

1. Another idea for inexpensive unlined notebooks are those 'chunky'
prebound memo books you can get from paper/school supply aisles of
stores like Rite Aid, Walgreen's, K-Mart, Wally World (W-M), etc.
They're about 2" x 2" x 1" and usually have some wacky 'kids'
cartoon characters on the cover like powerpuff kids or speed racer,
stuff like that. Some are just blank, bright colors with 'Mead'
stamped on it somewhere. They run about $1 - $2 each and you can
easily cover them with contact paper or decoupage, duct tape, camo
tape--something like that. The nice thing about them, though, is
they've got about 100 pages in them--so 200 sides of pages for
stamping in! The bad thing about them, though, is that they're
fairly thick and don't fit in many 'smaller' type letterbox
containers. If you're lucky, sometimes you can find these in the
clearance section of these stores.

2. Another type of letterboxing logbook I've come across frequently
are those spiral bound index card 'books'--folks carefully remove
the metal spine from it by 'unwinding' it from the pages, cut the
pages in half and/or break the pages up into 2-4 piles, cut
the 'spine' in half, carefully rethread the two spines through the
holes in the piles of the index cards. It looks very professional
and you can even add nice 'covers' to your front page by simply
punching holds in the top of some cardstock/cardbook and rethreading
them in with the other pages. I guess this is hard to describe
online but it's easier to follow if you see one out there--worst
case, just study a spiral bound notebook you have lying around,
imagine cutting it in half and the concept should come to you after
a while.

> I wonder if writing more straightforward clues is what most people
> prefer (or if people are just more inclined to comment on
> letterboxes when they feel something is 'out of whack' with the
> clues/trail/indicators/LB, etc)?

I think you might be right on both counts. Last week I actually got
an e-mail from someone who seemed FURIOUS at me for not just saying
where one of my walking tour boxes was. All that jumping through
hoops and such--didn't want no part of that. It was kind of funny,
in a sad sort of way, and I tried to explain that my walking tour
boxes weren't meant to be drive-bys. Anyone finding them should
expect to WORK for it!

But if you look at the boxes that tend to get the most visitors--
it's the easy drive bys that require little or no hiking and nothing
but straight-foward clues. There's nothing wrong with that (despite
e-mails of fury when I don't follow such conventions!), but I expect
to lose a certain percentage of the 'letterboxing audience' when I
don't write clues for the lowest common denominator. Each box has
an intended audience--whether implied or otherwise. Some people, I
think, forget that any given box is not meant for everyone, and they
are the ones most likely to complain about not liking a particular
style of clue or whatever.

But then, who cares? If somebody doesn't like a particular style of
clue, they shouldn't go looking for that box in the first place.
Give 'em hell, Bethany! ;o)
