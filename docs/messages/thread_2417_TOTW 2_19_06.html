From: (Doublesaj@aol.com) | Date: 2006-02-20 00:39:35 UTC-05:00
Very good question, Funhog. We all want to have our boxes last for a good
long time after going through the time and expense of creating a wonderful box
for our fellow 'boxers to enjoy. But when looking for good places for
planting a box, one must also think about where NOT to plant.

It's so important that we have not invaded anyone's 'personal space'.
Putting it another way, if there is paraphernalia suggesting homeless people are in
the area, forget planting a box there. Likewise if there are signs of a spot
used for high-schooler's parties or covert drinking activity by
anybody--bottles, cans, shopping carts, etc., don't plant a box there!

We also avoid railroad cars, maintenance areas, creeks, rivers or ANYPLACE
where there's a chance that water will flood it, or areas with high security.

We want a place were people are allowed to go but maybe not so many choose
to go. Little known hiking trails with wonderful views or something historic
or interesting to discover are our favorite places to plant and to find.

We'll get off our soapbox now. . .

Re: TOTW 2/19/06

From: lesliekusz (lesliekusz@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-02-20 19:49:50 UTC
Your soap box is much appreciated. :o) One of the boxes I couldn't find was in an area
which had been, I think, pristine, but had obviously been recently taken over by some very
incosiderate people--there were so many snack food wrappers, cans, and broken bottles
that I was actually afraid of getting hurt, so ended up not searching the entire area.

One thing that I'd suggest is that people ask themselves a few questions before placing a
box in any high traffic areas. Some of the urban ones are great *because* they are in high
traffic areas, of course; but I think one needs to ask the question, "Could anyone come up
with a good reason for being here OTHER THAN letterboxing?" Obviously if you leave one
in a shop with the owner or whatever, you can be subtle about getting the box. I found
one, though, that was in a sort triangular area between residential streets that didn't have
much cover, and I kept thinking, "What reason could I give for being here, if asked?" I was
careful, but if anyone happened to be looking out their windows, they could easily have
seen me.

I'd personally also ask the question, "Will someone be glad they came here?" Most of the
letterboxes I've found have been placed in locations where the answer to that question
would be a resounding "YES!" but there have been a couple where I felt like, "Why the heck
did they plant a letterbox HERE?" Not a complaint, really, just an observation.

I'm also thinking about Poison Ivy's great letterbox ranking system (which can be found on
Mark and Sue Pepe's blog). One of the things he mentions in this incredibly funny bit is a
box which has instructions that take you all over the place, but ends up being 20 feet from
where you parked your car. Those kind of boxes make me start foaming at the mouth
after I've found them, especially if I've gone past many really great hiding places that
would have made more sense.

Before I get off of *my* soap box, I'd also suggest that planters consider whether
something mentioned in the clues is going to be there later--small trees and the like
might end up being casualties of winter storms in certain areas, so keep them out of the
clues unless they are extremely sturdy. And if you are including cryptic clues, make sure
to spell things properly...&-)

Anyway, that's just my opinion, and I hope I haven't offended--just want to get the
maximum fun out of this pursuit.

Thanks!

Re: TOTW 2/19/06

From: dvn2rckr (dvn2rckr@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-02-23 02:46:35 UTC
Ref Letterboxing vs Geocaching: The difference between letterboxing
& geocaching is that the stamp is absolutely 'critical' to the
letterbox. If the stamp goes missing, it does not count as a 'find'
(referring to the LBNA FAQ for what constitutes a find, plant or
exchange) as an impression of the stamps can not be exchanged. If
the logbook goes missing but the stamp remains, you can always stamp
your signature stamp on a sheet of paper and leave it in the
letterbox and still get 'credit' for the find.

::

In geocaching, it still counts as a find if you locate an empty
container--you've still essentially 'found' the cache and can log it
as such--you can even do some quick maintenance by adding some paper
for a new notebook and toss in a few trade items and it's back in
business. That's why it's so frustrating for letterboxes when the
stamps go missing (not to mention the extra effort it takes to
recarve the stamp, return to the location and replace it). It's
rare when folks are able to 'repair' a letterbox on the spot when
the stamp goes missing because folks generally don't carry carving
material with them on the trail and if the stamp goes missing, they
usually have no idea what the original looked like. So getting it
back in 'business' is more difficult.

::

Yes, there are 'problem' participants from both hobbies--believe me,
if you've participated in these hobbies for more than a few weeks
you'll experience it.

::

I'm almost game to recommend that geocaches become 'hybrid'
letterboxes that incorporate stamps just so the awareness level of
the importance of the stamp remaining in the container is elevated.
If you delve into the genesis of geocaching you'll see that its
original intent was to be a new, more technical twist on
letterboxing (study the letterboxing-usa yahoogroup's early messages
to see this). What happened though was a fallout between
the 'founding fathers of US letterboxing' and the 'father of
geocaching' (who at one point was a letterboxer) regarding
who 'controls' or 'owns' the clues of planted letterboxes and
whether they should all be incorporated into the 'then newly
emerging geocache database.' The disagreement over this is was
created the schism between the hobbies and geocaching morphed into
what it is today--completely separating itself from the 'stamp'
aspect of letterboxing.

::

As both a letterboxing & geocaching enthusiast (who's planted &
found many letterboxes & geocaches), I try to raise awareness about
both hobbies on both sides of the aisle whenever I'm afforded the
opportunity. I challenge parties on both sides of the aisle to do
the same. I've even had great success working with geocache owners
in creating hybrid geocache/letterboxes along the way, too. So it
is possible to happily co-exist (& even cross-polinate for that
matter!)

::

Personally, though, I've had more problems with stamps disappearing
from our letterboxes that were eventually found in geocaches when
unknowing cachers swapped them out for coins or bits of polished
glass/stones than I've experienced with missing 'logbooks' from our
geocache. I have discovered remnants of hitchhiker letterboxes
and 'resident letterbox' stamps in geocaches, though--and only
recognized them as such because we'd previously found the said HH or
the resident letterbox and it instantly caught our eye.

::

This commentary is not meant as an inflammatory response. It seems
lately, however, that just expressing an opinion on any of these
boards incites some sort of angry response. As if this board is a
tinderbox or something. Sad! :( I'm hoping this 'quick-to-anger'
tendency is a result of cabin-fever and not anything else because
this board used to be our 'refuge' from all of that bickering that
was so common on the letterboxing-usa board.

::

Please view this response as just an attempt to encourage both
letterboxers and geocachers to individually do their parts to raise
awareness of the different hobbies amongst their friends from the
respective communities so as to reduce the friction the two hobbies
that is obviously occurring. Truly there are much more important
things in life than to quarrel over things like this. Obviously
participants in both hobbies share similar passions for exploring
outdoors and following some sort of clues to a 'surprising' result.
It's odd that it's so difficult for the two 'bodies' to get along.
And no--the previous posts weren't 'flameworthy'--check out the
letterboxing-usa board from late August 02-October 02 and a few
other nuclear-fallout-episodes after that. Those were not 'glory'
times WRT to this hobby. Just remember though that not everything
you see in this hobby is what it really is--some of those fallings-
out were 'baited' and folks actually fell for it.

dlphinus
