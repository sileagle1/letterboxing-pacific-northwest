Hi All!

I use all kinds of carving material. PZ Cut is wonderful for getting
great detail, though I've found sometimes it doesn't take the ink very
well. Sometimes it needs to scotchbrited or sanded to give the material
some tooth to adequately take the ink. It is thin. Sometimes I mount
it and sometimes I don't. I really like low-maintenance materials.

I use the "Pink Stuff" called Speedy Stamp, I believe, as well. It's
pretty good. A bit floppy, but nice to carve on, too. It gets pretty
good detail. I've only found it at Michael's and only after a real
search on my own. You'll get no help from the hired staff there. For
that reason I detest shopping there and avoid it like the plague. If
there is another source for Speedy Stamp I'd rather go there.

Mostly I tend to use Nasco Saf T Kut. Many people find it to be crumbly
and difficult to get detail with. I tend to carve bold images, for the
most part, and since it was one of the first materials I started carving
on way back in the early 90's I'm very comfortable with it. It's also
thicker than either PZ Cut or Speedy Stamp and it is more economical.
It costs less than $2 for a 4 x 6 sheet at Art Media in Portland.

MarsCarve or Master Carve are wonderful mediums. Delightful to carve
with. They get good detail and are very thick. They are also pretty
expensive, so, while I do carve with it at times and have some stock of
it at home, I rarely use it. Just for special occasions, I guess.
Whatever that means.

A tried and true inexpensive material that is also good is Dollar Tree
erasers. Sometimes you can get them in packs of one large and three
small erasers. AND...at only a buck per pkg! I know these are good and
work well and are cheap, cheap, cheap. I think The Fleshman's
One-Eyed-Willie stamp was carved with this material, if I'm not
mistaken. If so, then great detail can be had on this medium, as well.
The large eraser is very thick.

I have also tried Plumbers Gasket, but don't care for it much. It is
easy to get (Home Depot or hardware stores carry it). It's inexpensive
to buy by a large sheet. It is high-maintenance to me, though. It's
tough to carve (more like linoleum without the backing) and it always
has to be sanded to be able to take the ink well. Mounting isn't
necessary, but the material is way thin. Like, much thinner than PZ
Cut, even. It is best used with very sharp tools.

That is my low down on most of the stuff I have used. Each material
holds different esteem and quality in my eyes. It depends on the
purpose, my mood, supply and circumstances which material I will use for
what. It's great to try all kinds of different carving medium to get
you used to it so you can carve on anything in a pinch. Preferences
are nice, but if you get too used to one type of material it's easy to
get stuck on it and not be able to carve well on anything else, or just
be disappointed when your carve doesn't turn out like you want because
you really want to be using something else.

There you have it. I don't claim to be an expert or an outstanding
carver, but I do have years of carving experience and plenty of
opinions! Take it as you may.

Best regards,

Amanda Briles
