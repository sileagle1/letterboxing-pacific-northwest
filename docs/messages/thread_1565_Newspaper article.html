Well, 'horrible' seems a bit harsh, but I agree that it is a misnomer
to say that letterboxing is 'all about' one or two aspects of the
hobby. It is about many different things to different people and at
different times.

For example, in my case, sometimes, the hiking aspect of it is very
important to me. That's when I try to find (or place) a box that is
out on a hiking trail. Other times, I'm in the mood for something a
little more domestic, like a quaint walk in the park with my nephew.
Then I really prefer to hunt (or plant) a nearby local box.

If I were going to make any attempt to simplify what it's 'all about'
to me, personally, I would have to say that letterboxing is about
sharing an experience with others. One person likes a location
enough to plant a box there. Later, other people come to that same
location trying to find it. In the process, they partake in the
enjoyment of the area. Then they add their name (stamp) to the list
(logbook) of people who have had the pleasure of sharing the
experience.

But that's just my opinion. We each have different opinions, none
more valid than another. As for me, I appreciate a beautifully
carved stamp, but that's not really important. I am also happy to
find a crudely carved stamp. I actually do care about the number of
stamps I collect, since each one is a keepsake of an experience I
shared with one or more other people. But, again, that's just my
opinion.

Overall, I thought the article was well-written, and I was glad to
see how much emphasis was placed on respecting the environment on
which we tread. Very nice.

Mitch, Der Mad Stamper

environment.
