Well, here's another look at it:

http://www.thenewstribune.com/opinion/story/5524869p-4976739c.html

I've never felt badly about supporting something I use. I have even
considered purchasing Oregon's pass, because I use their parks. There have
been so many visible improvements in the 4 parks on the island, here, that
are a direct result of access to these fees. It's interesting to me to
watch our state's priorities. If the fees are repealed, I think we will pay
a different price for our parks, in the form of reduced programs for our
children, increased vandalism by people who just don't care (now that they
don't have to pay $5 to drink beer in the backwoods), and possibly closures.
The other thing that concerns me is how any possible replacements of the
income, if found, will be apportioned. I believe the current system has
each park directly benefiting from at least a portion of the fees they bring
in. If the fees are repealed, some of those lovely, tiny parks that we are
so fond of are likely to have to claw, scratch, and bite to obtain funding
that will naturally be likely to go to larger, more popular parks like our
Deception Pass.

OK. Off my soapbox, now. :-P

From: dvn2rckr (dvn2rckr@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-02-21 16:33:00 UTC
Well, I purchased the annual pass 3 years in a row--even though a
family member (with whom we often go hiking) gets it for free for
being a disabled veteran. What ticked me off about it though is
that as soon as the parking fee went into place they closed the park
nearest us (Tolmie SP & many others around the state) more often
than it was open so it was almost a moot point for us. That was odd-
-they charge the fee then closed a bunch of parks despite it!

The ironic effect of the parking fee, though, is that folks refused
to visit the parks because of the exorbitant fee, which in turn
caused attendance to drop, which in turn reduced the additional
funding parks received from the state (as the funding is based on
attendance numbers). It was self-defeating. I don't mind the fee
but I wish the daily fee was much lower than $5! Since the fee went
into effect we rarely saw more than 1 or 2 vehicles in the area
state parks when we visited. Not good for future funding from a
budgeting perspective. I'd support $1-$2 for parking and perhaps a
$25-$30 annual pass with a discount toward camping fees for those
pass holders--but they didn't take anything like that into
consideration. It was less expensive to not purchase a park pass
and camp your way through the system because camping still gave you
a daily pass to the parks for the same price that annual pass
holders had to pay even though they'd already spent $50 on the pass.
Frustrating anyway you look at it.

I didn't post the info to provoke an argument. Just to let folks
know what was going on with the park pass in the event they don't
read the area newspapers.

For anybody interested in WA State Park Policy, join the Washington
State Parks - Policy Feedback Group (a policy group of which I've
been a member for the past few years) and voice your opinions!
Contact Mr. Don Powell [Don.Powell@parks.wa.gov] to be added to the
group listserv.

Enjoy!

From: Susan Mohn (twilight2001@earthlink.net) | Date: 2006-02-21 08:53:46 UTC-08:00
Remember, the park fees are a very recent thing and were instituted
because of a deficit in the state bank account -- now that there's a
surplus, the funding mechanism is back in place -- the fees (which have
been HUGELY unpopular) are being removed.

The amount of grief the legislature has taken over this "twice tax" (as
it's been touted) is unbelievable. The fact is, taxes are paid and
state parks are to be maintained out of that fund. The extra fee was
supposed to be just for a short time to cover a deficit in the state
fund.

I'm not a) sure it will be a problem at all or b) sure they had a real
choice in the matter, given we're coming up on mid-term elections...
