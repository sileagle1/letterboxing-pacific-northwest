From: funhog1 (funhog@pacifier.com) | Date: 2004-05-09 04:36:12 UTC
Everyone has their own way. I like being able to stuff a little piece of paper into my
pocket if someone happens along the trail. I always like to try to be inconspicuous.
Generally, I only print the clues that I intend to use during the current boxing venture
but there is a standing collection of ones that I want to search out that are in my
backpack at all times.

I do know of one letterboxing day that was saved by a Palm Pilot. The sun had set and
the only available light was the eerie glow of a Palm. It was the one most necessary
tool for finding that pesky Rock Creek Troll. I have also received a letterbox update
directly from the site of one of my boxes in IA via wireless internet connection. Very
Cool Geekiness!!!

As for wasting paper, I am a recycling maniac. First, I NEVER print the clues directly
from the LbNA site. I always cut and paste into a word processing app, make the font
smaller, eliminate stuff I don't need and then print on the back of junk mail or other
used paper. Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Funhog

From: dvn2rckr (dvn2rckr@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-05-09 16:05:53 UTC
As we travel fairly regularly, we keep a binder in the car with
clues to letterboxes from the state, organized by region. We also
have binders for surrounding states as we sometimes learn about
trips at the last minute and don't have a whole lot of time to spend
tracking down clues.

For example: we've got a WA binder--broken down mostly by counties
and then a section for mysteries (unsolved--otherwise the solved
ones go in their respective sections). We've got an OR binder that
is twice the size of WA's. We also have a CA & ID binder (combined)
because it only includes Northern CA. Then we have a VA/MD/DC
binder that also contains some clues for PA, NJ & NY as that's
another area we get to at least once a year. Then there's the
Cincinatti binder (check out the website and you'll see it needs its
own GIANT binder thanks to one Mr Franzsolo) although this isn't a
place we get to very often.

I thought about dumping clues to my PDA but haven't gotten around to
doing it yet.

As for the Lion Letterbox--if it's the one near the playground since
there was another box in that park, too. The clues are not very
specific and I doubt you'll ever hear from the kid who placed the
letterbox. To avoid looking VERY SUSPICIOUS as an adult playing in
a playground, we found the box buried about 1-1/2" deep in the bark
beneath the slide. As you stand beneath it (the wooden platform
just before it connects with the metal of the slide) facing the side
where you climb up it (back to the part of the slide where it would
take you once you slid down), the box was buried on the left side
almost directly beneath the joint where the wood and the metal meet
(if I remember correctly). The clues were awesome up until that
part--as it wasn't very specific as to where in the bark one needed
to dig. Talk about a needle in a haystack!!!

Oh, going back to clue binders. I also keep a section in each
binder with park maps, newspaper articles of local areas of interest
for letterboxes I've considered placing in the region, etc. This
way, when you visit the area, you've got some 'ammo' to help come up
with some interesting clues if you decide to place a last minute
letterbox while you're there. I also have an entire kit of
letterbox supplies (including pre-built letterboxes minus the stamps
& finished logbooks) that I carry in the car for all
those 'emergencies'. ;)

Hope this helps,

From: Dave Whitcher (fudrick@comcast.net) | Date: 2004-05-09 12:16:41 UTC-07:00
I also cut and paste the clue into a word document, delete all but the
specifics, then choose a font size so it fills one page. Not seeing so well
especially in the dark and brush and etc., the largest font possible allows
me to see it at a glance, rather than staring at a sheet of paper trying to
read the 8 point font, making it obvious I am trying to read something.

On the back of the sheet of 8-1/2 x 11 paper I put a map. Many places have
online maps of the park or the area or the city. Using the clues I can
track where it likely is, saving time and confusion while searching. (also
helps when I get lost :-) If not a site specific map, an area map to get to
the starting point.

I fold the paper so I am looking at the specific clues I am following, so it
is not an 8-1/2 x 11 sheet, but a 2-3/4 x 8-1/2 sheet, which can quickly
slide into a pocket when necessary.

Fudrick
