As to # of Letterboxes -- I figure as long as they're still being
visited, it's not too many :).

Ease (or difficulty) of climb, urban/not urban, close to town/miles away
-- these all add into it. There are 6 boxes (that I know of) at St.
Edwards. Could it host more? Probably -- but 6 is the current load.
All are being visited. There are 9 more up at Bastyr (ok, arguably the
same property) -- but those 9 are there for a very specific reason
(compass practice for Y camp and any other beginners who want the
practice). They are very close together because the compass work is the
point (not the hike). They are on trails that could (almost) handle a
stroller all the way -- so it's great for families that have both much
younger and much older folks who want to participate.

So that's 15 in (arguably) 1 park. Too many? Apparently not as they're
all still getting visits :).

There's my 2 cents...

> How many boxes in a given area is too many? Can there actually be
too many?

In the event of a multi-letterbox series, I prefer longer treks
between multiple letterboxes in a series--ie enough to make it worth
putting the 50lb baby backpack (baby & 'stuff' incl) back on in
between events. I've also grown to prefer outings of 1-3 letterboxes
within a series--because any more than that seems to be overwhelming
(with letterboxes spaced about a 1/2 to 1 mile apart--mostly because
I'm in the hobby for the exercise & the brain exercise of trying to
successfully follow the clues) and I start to find the stamping in
aspect of it rather cumbersome if the distance between two boxes
doesn't even elevate your heart rate.

Yes, places can become saturated with boxes. I'm sure saturation is
defined totally differently by various people.

CT = saturation

PNW = pleasantly sporadic with pockets of accumulated
condensation/puddles

Doesn't Dartmoor set the definition, though? What are the current
estimates for Dartmoor? I've heard that within a 200 sq mile area
there are around 2,000-5,000 known letterboxes (with several hundred
more 'unpublished' ones that this paeon will never have the privilege
of learning about). Using that as a guide, then CT is barely
moist...

However, if the goal of a letterbox is to teach somebody something
significant about the area or to introduce them to something they
ordinarily (without knowledge of the area or letterboxing) would not
have known about the region, then that drives me to personally place
a letterbox. Of course, in these comments I know I have a few boxes
that don't meet these criteria but, for the most part, that's what
drives me to place a letterbox in a region. Generally, that's why I
prefer to place historically based letterboxes (or at least that's
what I strive to do--if I accomplish that, then that's another story).
