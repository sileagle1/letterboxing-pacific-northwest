the unwritten rules

From: TeamWARE (letterboxing2004@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-08-03 08:57:54 UTC-07:00

I'll put my 2cents in about the "no rules" side of
this hobby. There are most definitely rules - they
may not be in book form but they are there and the
minute you break one you are busted.

There are a lot of veterans in this hobby who will let
you know if you're out of line. At the same time the
integrity of this hobby is at state when people start
breaking these unwritten rules and therefore someone
has to make sure people stay in line - for the most
part.
It's just like having a Daddy who keeps you on the
straight and narrow until you're old enough to begin
making your own decisions and understanding the impact
of those decisions affects not only you but everyone
around you and those who come after you.

I had to look at this hobby in that way as I was an
overzealous boxer last year who wanted to conquer the
world AND all the letterboxes - simple and complex -
in one fell swoop. I also saw some things "needing
assistance" and the mother in me stepped right into
action. YIKES!!!! I was too new to be making
parental decisions about other people's "babies".

So, my suggestions are simple and few:

Find it, enjoy it, and put it back so it isn't seen
and can be found again.

Fix it if you can with what you have on hand then let
the box parent (the placer) know about the damage and
what you did.

Do not remove anything from any box unless it is a
first finder or a hidden clue. If something is wet,
moldy, or full of porn sayings - alert the box parent
so they can "choose" whether or not to do something
about it. NOTE - offer to assist if they are unable
but never say you're going to do it.

Finally, don't use someone else's clues as a jump
point for yours nor nab their verbage for yours. BE
CREATIVE! We want to see your own flare and style. We
already know the flare of the vets - so strut your own
style and you may end up in the end as one of those
who is still kicking around in a few years.

Okay - that may have been a buck worth....sorry.

LILY

I found a box completely destroyed a few weeks ago, on a 10-mile
hike, lots of elevation gain. The log was soaked. I could have
replaced the log, the box, and dried/mailed the 4 logged-in pages to
the owner, who doesn't live nearby. I realize that maintenance is
his responsibility, but I could have saved him a trip and such a
long hike... I was only the 2nd person to log in in 2 years, the
4th since it was planted in 2002 or 2003.

I guess it would have been nice to save the owner a trip and a long
hike, that's all. In the end, I decided it wasn't my place to take
the log, but it truly was useless, nearly unreadable.

From: funhog1 (funhog@pacifier.com) | Date: 2005-08-04 04:28:44 UTC
I agree that it would've been a nice thing to do. I have
taken a soaked book home and dried it out, later
returning it to it's home. If a book is totally waterlogged,
I bet most placers would want you to take it out of the
box, dry it and send it to them as long as you left a r
eplacement in the letterbox.

Recently, I received an email about one of my boxes
in the East. The finder discovered the book soaking
wet, removed it from the plastic baggy and then left
it in the box to mold. I am only able to get there once
a year for maintenance. I would've much preferred that
they took it with them, returning it to me. This way, it
will be useless by the time I get there. I, for one, do
believe in taking a log with me if that's the best first aid
option. I would never keep it but I would try to preserve
it. Funhog
