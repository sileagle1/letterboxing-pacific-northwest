From: Jana J. Riska (jjcadenza@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-04-27 08:56:15 UTC-06:00
I trespassed once on a closed down railway line in Paris that I shouldn't
have gone to for two boxes. It was so pitch black; I couldn't see anything
but what my flashlight covered. The tunnel ended up feeling like it was
about a mile long. It probably wasn't quite that long, but it was close to
that. It was only as I got partway in, all alone, that it dawned on me that
nobody in the entire world knew where I was and I was staying all alone in a
strange country. If anything were to happen to me, there wasn't anyone that
would notice when I didn't return. I had to climb up these unbelievably
high ladders to find the spots, and then to add insult to everything, both
boxes were missing. The built-in ladders in the sides of the tunnel were so
high that I began to worry about my legs giving out underneath me and me
falling. If I had, I would have been very seriously injured. I repeatedly
considered turning around the whole time, but every moment I "just knew" the
box would be around the bend and I had come that far, it seemed like a waste
to not get the letterboxes.

When I returned, I was so eager to get out of that place that I just chose
to not worry about the fact that there was a group of guys approaching on
the other side of the fence that I had to hop. After they saw me, they came
over and believe it or not started threatening me and acting like I needed
to pay them to keep from getting me in trouble for being in that spot. They
all sort of made a circle around me. It was so scary--this group of men and
me all alone! They were clearly threatening me in French with a couple of
broken English phrases thrown in. I managed to get away by playing the
"dumb American" (sometimes there are a few advantages to looking like an
obnoxious, stupid tourist. It's the only time I was grateful for our
reputation with the French). I kept acting like I couldn't understand what
they were trying to say to me. "I'm sorry, I don't speak any French
(shrugging). I don't understand what you are saying!" I kept repeating it
until they looked utterly frustrated, and then I turned away, looking like I
regretted my ignorance, and walked as quickly away as I dared. I was
petrified that they would follow, but the neighborhood was busy enough, I
guess it wasn't worth it to them. I can't tell you how relieved I was!!

This experience taught me one of my most valuable lessons--no letterbox is
worth putting yourself in danger. I was incredibly stupid that day and all
for the sake of finding a box. I don't know what I was thinking going out
alone with no way of anyone knowing where I would be, and then I took such
stupid risks and even trespassed on closed property to find it. I might not
have pushed it, but all of my other attempts at finds in France had failed.
I just wanted one find at least. I shouldn't have cared that much. I was
very lucky to be okay in the end.

It also taught me to be mindful of who was planting. Most of the
letterboxes I looked for in France were from the same person that did this
one. There were a couple I didn't even attempt, even though I got there and
could clearly see the spot where the letterbox should be hidden. One wanted
me to climb a high wall of a hotel with only finger-sized cracks spider-man
style. I have NO idea how she planted that thing!! The other was after the
whole railway incident. I got there and saw that I would have to climb up
on this big wall that was a fence for someone's personal property and reach
up into a high lamppost. After everything else, I looked at it for a bit,
turned around, and went back to my hostel, never having found a letterbox in
Paris. It just wasn't worth it!

Luckily there was one that I looked for that was great. It turned out to be
missing, but I had so much fun looking for it that I was okay with it. I
spent the day in an amazing spot. That one wasn't planted by the dissused
railway chick. I will not look for another of her boxes when I move to
Europe!! I think she's planted all over.

http://groups.yahoo.com/group/letterbox-usa/message/32579

I posted notes on this on the big list and on the AQ France board, hoping to
warn some others. As far as I know the boxes are still listed, even though
this was three years ago that I tried to find them. It was a crazy story,
but it makes for a fun one to recount now at least! (-: I may not have got
a find, but I have a fairly interesting adventure to recount!

-Cadenza

Holy crap! Now I want to hear the port-a-potty story!! That is tooo funny!

Yeah, I definitely learned my lesson with this one. I think what taught me
the lesson was the fact that when I started, I had NO IDEA I would be in for
such an adventure. My biggest debate was "should I jump this fence." I
knew I probably shouldn't, but decided to since I had spent the day boxing
and found nothing. I pushed it for the sake of finding something.

I thought that would be it. I knew I needed a flashlight, but thought the
letterbox would be about 20 feet in the tunnel. Nobody was around. It
didn't look unsafe. I didn't picture a "ladder" being something that could
get me killed if I fell. There was no indication that anyone would threaten
me. I just faced the moral dilemna about should I go against my nature and
trespass a bit on property that looked safe and was clearly not being used
for anything that I thought would be problematic. I hadn't even thought
about my being alone because I was in a city. I don't really hike alone,
but in cities there are people everywhere. I thought I would be fine. It
just goes to show . . .

But yeah, it's a fun story to remember now that I'm okay!

Oh, I forgot other, more normal lengths I've gone to. How about
letterboxing in Salt Lake City after the biggest snowstorm in years left 3-4
of snow in one day! We had come all the way, we'd be darned if we went all
that way and didn't find the boxes. One of them was a bit tricky tough
because the snow had completly uprooted and pulled down the big tree it was
planted by. Amazingly, we still managed to find it! We were very wet in
the end after treading into thigh high snow in many spots, but we sure
enjoyed those boxes and I still smile when I see the stamps!

Cadenza
