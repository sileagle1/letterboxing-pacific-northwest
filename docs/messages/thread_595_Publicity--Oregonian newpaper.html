Publicity--Oregonian newpaper

From: (PNWEXPLR@aol.com) | Date: 2003-08-14 19:52:33 UTC-04:00
Hello all,

The result of Heather and my visit with a local reporter is in today's
Clackamas County Weekly section of The Oregonian. Thanks to all of you who replied
a few weeks back when I asked whether we should talk with this reporter or
not. Consensus was that it was best the article include the input of actual
LBers.

The good news for those who are unhappy with media publicity about our hidden
hobby is that this section of the Oregonian is only included in the Clackamas
County distribution area. (The masses of Multnomah and elsewhere are still
unaware...). The reporter did a fine job, mostly focusing on the
family/kid-friendly aspects of LBing (the article is in the "Education and Family" section
of the newspaper). Heather and I repeatedly stressed the low-impact to the
environment message that so many of you asked us to relay. It was boiled down
to this paragraph: "Lichen and Yost said letterboxers have one rule that they
insist everyone follow--whenever visiting a letterbox or placing a letterbox,
make sure the area is left the same way it was before a box was placed there."
Well, I might have liked more empathsis, but I was glad to see it in there.

In fact, the only thing I really have to complain about is that the photo
that accompanies the article includes all the Yosts--but not my dear little Wild
Child! (Oh well, I'll take comfort in the fact that she received extra
attention in the Stamp Camp photos Funhog kindly posted.)

Here are a few quotes pulled from the article:
"Letterboxing is like going on a regular hike, but you follow clues and
directions to find a rubber stamp," Megan said. "I like doing it because you have
to act secretive whenever you find the letterbox."

"I like reading the clues and figuring them out," Hallie said. "Some clues
are really hard and some aren't."
"What's really fun is when the parents are stumped and the kids find it,"
Lichen said.

"Part of the allure of letterboxing has been its secrecy," Lichen said. "Not
many people in the Northwest know about it, which adds to the furtive fun and
the sense of being part of a secret society."

Finding the box is the "icing on the cake"...The real treat is the experience
of being outside together "in some lovely places, stretching your legs and
your mind."

If anyone wants to read the article but is outside of the Clackamas County
distribution area, give me a shout and I'll see what I can do about either
including it in an email or snail-mailing you a copy.

--Wild Woman
