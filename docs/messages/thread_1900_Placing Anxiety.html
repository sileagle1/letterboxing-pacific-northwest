From: Tina (tinarie@msn.com) | Date: 2005-02-04 23:49:34 UTC

I understand your concern about your letterboxes. They tend to become
our "babies" so to speak and letting them go is somewhat stressful.
Beleive me, the more you place, the easier it is to let go. I know
there are a lot of letterboxers out there that feel the same way that
you do and they tend to treat the letterboxes that they find like one
of their own and take good care of it. They are also very good at
contacting the placer and letting them know that their baby is fine
and in some cases that there needs to be some repair (which they
generally take care of themselves) or if it goes missing. Just make
sure that the box is hidden well and that is pretty much all that you
can do. Thank you for taking care of other boxes that you find. I am
sure that the owners appreciate all that you have done.
All I can say is, just jump right in and hide your boxes and see what
happens. You will be surprised at how long some can stay hidden from
view. Happy Planting!

From: Steven Sheeley (rayvenhaus@lbworld.org) | Date: 2005-02-04 16:07:09 UTC-09:00
Flutterby,

All I can say is this on that score.... When we placed our first
letterboxes, it was in a place that was very close to home, like less
than 5 miles away. We planted a series called the Snow Dogs and we
checked them every day, then twice a week, then once a week, then
once a couple of weeks, then once a month, then.....

One day, after they'd been there for awhile, we went to check on
then, having quite some time pass since our last check. At that point
we discovered that we had lost approx 40% of the boxes we planted.
It was then that I decided to treat letterboxes as children and
prepare them as best as I could for the real world and then send them
off on their own.

Nothing will ever replace a lost/damaged/misplaced letterbox. This
is a chance you take by placing it out in the wild for others to
search for. Even sending them through the Mail to specific people
does not stop them from becoming lost, as we came to learn on the ill
fated Aztec Gold PLB.

Take pleasure in the crafting, take pleasure in the finding, take
pleasure in the simple fact that you're outside doing something and
seeing something you prolly wouldn't have seen had it not been for
letterboxing and remember this one simple thought......


Even if your golden rule is to do unto other's letterboxes as you'd have
them do unto yours, many won't. It's a sad and simple fact in many
aspects of life and even more prevalent here in this hobby for some
strange reason.


We check our boxes whenever we get a chance to. If they are there, we
are overjoyed. If they are missing, then we have a moment of silence
and we move on. (I'm kidding, we just move on! (grin)) We plan the
replacement, we get to search a new location, we get to research and
build new clues, we find the silver lining and we embrace it. If you
worry about the what ifs, you'll never get out and plant one. And we
plant so others can find, so they'll plant so we can find, do we not?

P.S. We keep an image of every stamp we've created, in our own little
stamp gallery we carry with us so we'll at least have that, if nothing
else, should it go missing.

> but my concern has been how often does one have to check on your
> placed letterboxes? I have anxiety about them being lost. Or not
> being able to get to them to check on them.

You don't actually ever HAVE to check up on your boxes. I've planted
some in rather remote areas (two in Africa jump into mind in my case)
that is likely to see the end of the earth before seeing me again.
Such boxes I "maintain" remotely based on feedback from people who try
to find the box. If I think the box is likely missing, I'll mark it
as such.

As for anxiety about losing boxes--it WILL happen. No box will last
forever. Eventually, somewhere, someday, your box will go missing.

There are things you can do to help reduce the likelihood of a box
going missing. The more remote it is, the less likely it'll go
missing. The fewer people who look for it, the less likely it'll go
missing. If you can hide it indoors behind a counter somewhere, it's
less likely to go missing.

But in the end, there's nothing you can do to guarantee the safety of
a letterbox, so try not to grow too attached to it. Losing a box gets
easier with experience as well. =)

-- Ryan

I have been fairly fortunate to have only lost one box. I thought it
was hidden well at in a crack in an enormous boulder covered in lots
of smaller rocks. it was in a beautiful spot at the end of a
magnificent canyon with with a river running through it, a popular
rafting river, on a sandy beach where I often stop to rest on rafting
and boat trips, well I didnt stop to think of the millions of other
rafters that do the same thing every day of the summer and assumed it
would be safe buried in a crack under rocks. I was wrong. But hey, no
biggie right? it wasnt the most wonderful carving Ive done, it was a
spur of the moment sitting-on-the-beach-frying-in-the-sun kind of
stamp of a chinook salmon. Its ok, its all part of letterboxing, Ryan
is right, the more remote of a place, the less likely it is to go
missing but sometimes I like to put boxes in busy places for the less
adventurous letterboxers who maybe cant hike as far as others. I keep
a stamped image of all my carvings so I will always have that :o) and
if I want to recreate the stamp, I have the original for a template.
