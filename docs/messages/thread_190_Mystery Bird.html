First thing that pops into mind is an escaped African Crowned Crane.
We have had escaped birds of different species ( including a Hoopoe!)
from a bird collection in Renton.

Great Blue Herons in breeding plumage have rust on the wing ( at the
elbow , the pointy bit at the front)

All of our state egrets are white and typically in eastern washington
with rare visits to w wash.

You might be discribing Cattle Egret which is quite rare here in
washington. However they are not BIG but somewhat the size of a
standing tall Crow. They do have rust highlights on their body which
is mostly white. Should be in your field guide.

Sktech the bird on paper, color some spots and send to me if you like.

Hoopoe

Sandhills are amazing birds and one of only two native North American
cranes ( whooping is the other)

There many other crane species in pan-Asia. Many of them are revered
for their fidelity to their mates. The mating dances of these birds
are wonderful to see. Ther are featured in many classic asian
artworks.

Othello , in eastern Washington sponsors the Sandhill Crane Festival
every spring. While numbers are not as great as the central flyway,
I had the experience of watching about 2000 birds approach up a long
vally and land on the cliff edge above us. We could hear before we
saw them and it was an awsome sight ( and sound)

There are no letterboxes in Othello but there are some near by ...
hint hint.

Sandhills are rare on the west side of the Cascades in Washington. I
did have a flock of 6 at the Kent Ponds one year. They were in Fall
migration and pushed on after 1 day. You can find them randomly in
the Spring on the west side of the Cascades in Oregon.

hoopoes are more likely to be in India right now, but any place you
hang your hat

Cheers
