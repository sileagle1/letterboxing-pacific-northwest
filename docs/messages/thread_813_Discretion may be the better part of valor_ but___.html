From: rscarpen (RiskyNil@pocketmail.com) | Date: 2003-10-15 16:58:14 UTC
> I get the distinct impression that letterboxing is divided into 2
> camps.

I don't really see it like that. I see it more as a spectrum where
each of your camps is on opposite sides of the spectrum, but then are
are plenty of boxers and boxes that fall between them. For instance:

1. Mischief is famous for her stunning stamps, but likes to place
them on easy hikes with simple clues.

2. I'm infamous for placing stamps that require long, strenuous
hikes, but if I'm the first person to start hiding boxes in an area,
I deliberately make them more accessible and use simple clues to
introduce new people to the hobby.

3. Der Mad Stamper can carve some INCREDIBLE stamps, but he's also
notorious for ugly foam stamps--claiming to have placed those so as
not to intimidate newcomers and show that a stamp doesn't have to be
a masterpiece to be worthy of a letterbox.

> Is there a place (a website maybe-tee hee) with the
> unspoken "rules", like plant no more than 5 boxes, post only
> challenging clues, don't post to the LPNW board more than once a
> month, don't ever call it a sitrep if you do, etc?

I've started trying to gather "unspoken rules" on my website at
http://www.geocities.com/rscarpen/Lb as various sorts of
documentation, but even then much of it is still debateable. For
instance, on my page about what makes a great letterbox great,
there's plenty of debate regarding what makes a good clue. My
favorite boxes tend to have elaborate clues that can be very
difficult to solve, but that wouldn't be a good idea for a box that's
meant for beginners or kids. In that case, a simple, straight-
forward clue would get the job done better.

I think--and I could be wrong--but it seems to me that the two camps
you see are the difference between "adult" boxes and "kiddy" boxes.
There's nothing inherently wrong with either type, but they certainly
are designed for two very different kind of audiences.

-- Ryan

From: pandora{HIB} (hispandora@phlsystems.com) | Date: 2003-10-15 10:09:26 UTC-07:00
Greetings Cami -



I am unsure if anyone else has answered this as I receive the group emails
in the digest format, but thoughts I would attempt to explain my perception
of the whole thing to you.



First of all I do believe that you are correct in your interpretation of a
difference in style in the letterboxing community. I think you will find
more subcategories than the ones you list however. For example there are
those that are very prone to using compass readings in their clues vs. those
that use paces, steps and clear concise directions. There are those make
you hike miles for one stamp and those that give you the stamp in the
beginning of a trail head and then allow you to decide how far along the
trail you wish to go from there. There are those that do very cryptic clues
and easily hid stamps and visa versa. There are those that do both. As for
carvings I would say that in that aspect also lays a vast variety of what is
done based some on time, some on potential and skill, and some on the
overall desire and purpose of the carver in question. Personally I do many
of the above things. My clues are sometimes cryptic; they are sometimes
straight forward and to the point. My carvings are sometimes varying
detailed, and sometimes, when carving something on the spur of the moment
they are simple. I even have some carvings that appear very childish in
artwork and style, as I had Iron`Bear's 7 year old daughter draw the image
and carved it exactly as she drew it. My Family Photo Letterbox for
example.



To answer your question I do not think there is anything wrong with being a
Newbie to the sport. As a matter of fact if you have been a member of the
group long you will notice I once proclaimed myself the permanent Newbie of
Letterboxing in an attempt to stick up for other Newbies out there. I think
that old time letterboxers welcome Newbies to the group quite openly; it
allowed them to actually hunt boxes rather than planting them all the time.
Just ask Ryan about the SLO area. Until last year Ryan was just about the
only one planting anything there. Then a group of enthusiastic Newbies came
along and now Ryan can hardly keep up with finding everything that is
getting planted out there. I think however you feeling and desire for a
website on how to behave in the letterboxing community, list wise, post
amount, language and so forth is a little dramatic. I don't mean that to be
offensive, I just think that basically it is all about common sense.
Perhaps I just had experience on other lists before joining this one and new
a little more of net etiquette so to speak however. In an attempt only to
help I will make the following suggestions. I am not saying you have done
any of the following, just responding to your questions.



1. Always make your subject line descriptive about your post so it can be
skipped over by those that are not interested.

2. No need to post a zillion messages a day to the board unless you are
responding to other emails. Try to put more than one thought in your posts
to condense a little and so that the list doesn't seem flooded with your
name in their email boxes.

3. Don't flame and attack on the message board, if you have a problem with
someone take it off the list and discuss it there so you don't make yourself
look like an ass in front of the rest of the group as well.

4. Don't just jump right, sit back awhile and see how the board flows, what
the other members do.



As to your other questions... I would say plant as many boxes as you like,
make the clues as hard or as easy as you desire, post site reps if you want
to just make sure to tell so in the subject line so that those that don't
want to read them can skip them. Keep in mind that if all your clues are
easy, all your stamps are simple, and you plant lots and lots of boxes, your
boxes may not appeal to the letterboxer looking to be a little more
challenged. Is there anything wrong with that? Nope, not at all. But then
don't piss in their cheerios if they feel that way either. That is their
right just as yours is to do what feels good and brings you joy.



Last but not least I would suggest that you don't post things you don't want
replies to, don't ask questions you don't want answers too, and don't ask
for opinions if you only want ones that agree with you.



As for Miss Janet's Rules -- I don't have her website handy, but if Ryan
reads your post or my reply he will probably pipe in and tell you how wrong
I am and how my opinions all go against Miss Janet's Rules, and he would be
the most educated in Miss Janet's List Conduct since she is a figment of his
imagination.



I bid you well,

pandora{HIB} - Iron`Bear's addicted letterboxer
