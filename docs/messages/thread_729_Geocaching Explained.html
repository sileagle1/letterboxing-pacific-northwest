> Instead of using specific clues to a box they use a GPS to take
> them to a specific grid coordinate that should lead them to
> the 'cache'. Sometimes their instructions for a box will include
> some more specific info like--once you're in the area, look for a
> large tree or an intersection of two paths, then SE of it is the
> cache behind a log or in a stump. Other times the clue is really
> just a grid coordinate--depending on the accuracy of their GPS' it's
> hit or miss finding the cache. Sometimes if trails aren't well
> marked in a specific area/park then geocachers may 'bushwhack' their
> way to a cache instead of following the recommended/established
> trail in the area. Once they locate the cache, they usually contain
> a logbook of some sort and then various prize/trinkets for which
> they must trade an item of comparable or greater value or intrinsic
> worth (often the first finder prize is $20 or a new CD or even gear
> from a GPS company since it's sponsored by those companies). A neat
> hobby since it is quite similar to letterboxing, I think it is even
> an offshoot of letterboxing, although I don't know all the specifics
> about how it 'spun off'--just the method of locating the cache is
> sometimes not as direct and may cause more damage to the environment
> than if they were to use direct clues such as many of the clues to
> letterboxes since letterboxing clues often incorporate the
> established trail in the context of the clues leading to a hiding
> spot that is typically just adjacent to the trail or a few steps
> away.
>
> I've come across several geocaches in our letterboxing adventure
> around the US and often make efforts to stamp in and to ensure it's
> doing well (the lingering Girl Scout in me) and even eventually
> contact the placer if I can identify who it is in the log. I even
> lurk on their message group. However, since my part-time employment
> often requires the use of technology & GPS's, what attracts me to
> letterboxing is the clue, escaping from the rigors of daily life
> (read technology), exploring nature/parks around me and learning
> about/researching local history. Personally, I find that more
> intriguing than looking for soggy Happy Meal toys around the forest
> (something I can do at any moment within the backseats of my car
> thanks to my 2 dear not-so-compliant children!). Regardless, if
> this hasn't answered your question, you can learn more at:
> http://www.geocaching.com/
>
> Have a great day and welcome to letterboxing! Can't wait to get the
> opportunity to look for letterboxes in your neck of the woods!!!
