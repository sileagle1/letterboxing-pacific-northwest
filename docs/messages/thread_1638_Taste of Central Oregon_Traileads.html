Taste of Central Oregon/Traileads

From: (paisleyorca@webtv.net) | Date: 2004-09-21 17:39:01 UTC-07:00
Hey All!

John and I went out for a beautiful end-of-summer stroll in a beautiful
city park. We took the clues for Taste of Central Oregon with us and
found this incredible letterbox placed by 4DToes. I do believe we will
have to get over the mountain to get more of the letterboxes placed by
this artistically-inclined clan. I just love John Fleshman's carving
style!!!

As for trailhead break-ins, it's really hit or miss and not necessarily
the destiny of one particular spot.

We've never been broken into while letterboxing since we started back in
2000. However, John's Jeep was broken into at Lolo Pass in Zig Zag
while we were off hiking. And my entire truck was physically stolen
right from the driveway of my apartment next to my neighbor's window in
my "safe" Laurelhurst neighborhood a few years after I first moved back
to Portland. It doesn't matter.

You can cry and be an alarmist and pout "Why me?" and what it will get
you is tons of sympathy because then you find out what a common
occurrence it is. Everyone will come up to you and say, "Dang! That's
terrible! When my car got broke into they took this and this and this"
And "They screwed up the lock so bad I couldn't even open the trunk till
I got it repaired when mine got vandalized" or "When it happened to me
they busted out the window to the extent..." etc. etc. etc. It is a
complete affront and violation of your private space when your vehicle
is busted into or stolen, but at the same time it's more a matter of
when, not if it will happen to your vehicle. If it hasn't happened to
you so far, then count your blessings!

Another thing you may want to consider...at least you have a car that
can be broken into. Some people are not so well off and don't even have
that much. We should count ourselves lucky to have material goods and
have our basic needs met, I think. Just something to think about.
There are lots who are way less fortunate. The things that really count
in life are the intangibles. We shouldn't get too bent out of shape
when money and things go fleeting out of our hands sometimes. At least
we had that money and those things at some point in our lives and if we
really want to it to be, it can be replaced.

Sometimes I think we should concentrate on what we can give rather than
what is taken from us. It can give us a sense of humility, empathy, and
an opportunity to be good and share with others. It comes back to us in
different ways and instead of our feeling sorry for ourselves over what
we've lost, we can give ourselves a chance to be empowered and
enlightened by the uplifting act of giving. Giving can negate what's
taken and replace a bad feeling with a good one.

Best regards,

Amanda Briles
The Paisley Orca

Your car can be broken into anywhere--not just the dinky trail
heads. I've seen cars that had been broken into in the parking lot
of an apartment complex in good neighborhoods. It's like the Paisley
Orca said--if you own a car, eventually, someone, somewhere, is going
to want to break into it. There are things you can do to help reduce
that risk including where you choose to park it, but in the end,
everything you can do may not be enough.

On the bright side, even as 'common' as break-ins are, they're still
relatively uncommon. I've parked at trailheads hundreds of times and
my car has never been broken into, including the trailhead where your
car was hit.

Suggestions about not leaving valuables in plain view in a car isn't
a condemnation against you, but rather a suggestion for how you and
other on this board might reduce risk of break-ins in the future.
You're solution (i.e. Don't go!) may not be an acceptable solution
for others on this board that still want to get the letterbox.

I'm willing to pay the price of a break-in for many years of happy
hiking on beautiful, wonderful trails. =) But I certainly try not
to make my car or anything into too appealing to thieves, either....

Lily,

I think you will quickly find on letterboxing lists that things you least
expect to cause a stir will cause a Stir. The other thing you will probably
find is that being the type that just has to have the last word will
probably get you no where fast. Not that you are, just an observation. I
don't think Paisly Orca meant any offense, in fact, having met her, I know
she didn't she is a very kind hearted and soft spoken individual.

At the end of the day Lily I think where you are sensing the argument from
is that a lot of us on the list feel that asking the place to be accountable
for your personal responsibility is a bit outlandish. Things like this
happen in life, they happen to everyone, when I lived in California I had my
house broken into 5 times.

I think the other point that people are trying to make that you are
overlooking, is that regardless of what the officer on the scene may have
told you... LOTS and LOTS of letter boxers have parked in the very same
place that you did, got the very same box you got, and not had their car
broken into. So asking a placer to change their clues to include a warning
because your car got broken into when 75 other people (that is a guess) had
no problems there is statistically invalid.

I agree with you absolutely that you are a free citizen who has a right not
to be violated, but you're preaching to the choir here. Nobody said what
happened to you was right, just that it could have happened anywhere,
anytime, anyplace. Everyone tried to give some suggestions on how to
prevent such things from happening in the future. No harm there, I would
personally rather have experienced hikers tell me what precautions they
take, then to have one incident scare me from a location and a letterbox,
doing that is a large injustice to the placer, and future finders... if you
know what I mean.

Pandora

From: Der Mad Stamper (letterboxr@aol.com) | Date: 2004-09-22 04:03:17 UTC
Lily, I could not agree more with Pandora's assessment of the
situation. I understand that you are rattled right now, and you feel
violated. That's understandable. But, there really did seem to be
an undercurrent in your messages suggesting that Funhog is somehow
partially responsible for what happened to you because she should
have warned you of the possibility of a break-in.

Just because the cops told you that break-ins had occurred there in
the past, doesn't mean Funhog was aware of this info. Nor does it
neccessarily mean that you are at a greater risk at that trailhead
than anywhere else.

We feel for you, because most of us have had similar things happen to
us... but we also feel for Funhog, and feel the need to jump to her
defense. That's why everyone is leaping onto this thread.

We all try to place our boxes responsibly, but please don't point a
finger at us if you manage to get hurt, scared, or ripped-off in the
process of looking for them. We should all take responsibility for
the risks we take.

I work at a college where parking lot break-ins occur several times
every semester. But I would never tell students that they shouldn't
attend college because of it. Sure, it's nice if instructors want to
remind students of the possibility, but it's not their responsibility.

Any time you leave your car unattended in a public place, you are
taking a risk. Any time you walk around by yourself in a secluded
area, you are taking a risk. This should be obvious.

Just to be on the safe side, maybe we should all put the following
notice at the end of our clues... Warning: The world is a dangerous
place!

Just to reiterate what Ryan said here about having your car broken into can
happen anywhere. I thought I would give you one of my own experiences. Quite
some time ago I was in downtown Portland with a friend of mine. We were
around the Pioneer Square area around lunch time. I drove a 1972 Chevy
stepside pick-up that was well - lets say built - and a very nice truck. We
were right around the corner from where I had parked my car when I thought I
heard my truck alarm going off. I turned and ran back and check it - where
to my amazement a man had the hood up trying to disconnect the alarm. The
police and ambulance had to be called - because for some strange reason he
decided that me yelling at him to leave my truck alone was something he was
actually going to be offended over. I must have really rattled him or
something because it certainly looked like he fell and broke both his knee
caps. [smiles slightly] My point to this little story is that tribulations
can occur anywhere - even in middle of the day on a busy street. Everyone
should always be as careful as possible - but at the same time not overly
careful that they are not able to live their life to their up most
happiness.

I bid you all well.
