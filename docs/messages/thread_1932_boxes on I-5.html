Yep, I had about a dozen go missing just like this (in urban area
parks) and resorted to the last ditch effort of pulling all the
clues to my other boxes in the area off of the internet. It seemed
to stop the problem before they vandalized them all but the mere
fact that it happened was VERY demoralizing. I stopped planting
boxes for a LONG while after it happened.

This is why so many folks are weary of introducing high school aged
kids to the hobby and the occasional newspaper article about the
hobby. Not necessarily out of fear of what the 'respectful' high
schooler or average newspaper reader would do to the box but really
more out of fear the 'respectful' high schooler would tell their
perhaps not so 'respectful' high schooler buddy about the box's
existence and then that not-so-respectful kid and his/her other
buddies would go out on a binge to hunt/vandalize/destroy boxes in
the area. On occasion, we've also had boxes filled with 'yucky'
stuff to which kids should not be exposed. Yes, unfortunately there
are some downsides to this hobby occasionally. Thankfully, it's not
that common.

That's why camouflaging boxes is very important and locating the
perfect hiding spot helps, too. Having a knowledge of where the
local geocache in the area is seems to help, too by trying to plant
the letterbox a great distance away from the geocache helps prolong
the lifespan of the letterbox as the geocachers who occasionally
stumble upon a letterbox don't have the precise hiding spot info for
the letterbox--they usually just leave it generally near where they
found it as their hobby doesn't require a specific hiding spot--just
a general grid square location.

That's also why I don't often leave 'sequential' clues in letterbox
series--for fear if one box accidentally falls into the wrong hands
then they'll have the clues to all the letterboxes in that series.
No fun finding that the first box in a series is missing and knowing
that that first box contained the clues to the next box and so on.
This is also a reason why I mix up my clues a bit, too. I try to
include directions, pace counts, azimuths, terrain/plant
identification and some other 'loophole' bit of knowledge (like an
untranslated foreign term) in the most of the clues we craft. Not
necessarily to be a pain but to make it a little more difficult to
find the box if you lack one of the necessary tools (compass, plant
id, prep research). Then, if for some reason the clues fall into
the wrong hands, they'll at least need a compass, a dictionary or
internet access to quickly find the box. Sometimes that
extra 'effort' requirement discourages folks from
damaging/vandalizing the boxes. They'll just seek out easier prey.

Fortunately, the hobby provides a lot more pleasure than the
downsides of it. One just needs to know to expect the bad stuff
occasionally so as to not get too disappointed when it happens.

Here's an example. We're in a totally different region of the
country at the moment and yesterday we found a letterbox/geocache
hybrid. The letterbox started out as a pure letterbox with specific
clues leading to it. Six months after it was planted it somehow
turned into a geocache as well. I'm not sure if the original
planter of this letterbox 'converted' it or if it was just adopted
by geocachers somewhere along the way. Somehow it also ended up on
the geocaching website as a geocache with a completely different
name than the letterbox name. The original planter is no longer
available and the letterbox has been adopted by another area
letterboxer who did so just to save it from being discarded during
the website transition a couple years ago. The original clues
leading to the letterbox say it should be some 30 steps (15 paces)
away from where we actually found it. The clues are rather specific
using plant and terrain identification in the clues but for some
reason the letterbox has 'migrated' some 30 steps (60+ feet) away
from its original hiding spot. Not sure why-- but letterboxers with
the letterbox clues may never find it--because they're looking for
specific landmarks/plants mentioned in the clues. Geocachers have
the grid coordinate from the entry in the geocaching page and once
their GPSr reads that grid then they sort of 'canvas' that general
area until they find the cache. Yesterday, we were just giving up
on this letterbox when I noticed the tell-tale letterbox/geocache
stick altar beneath a bush. Curiosity got the better of me and I
looked beneath the 'unnatural' arrangment of sticks and bark and
found what turned out to be the letterbox. Reading through the
logbook, it had six months worth of letterboxers sign into it right
after it was planted back in 2002, then a flurry of geocachers
signed in/left cards/trinkets, and an occasional letterboxer stamp
appeared beyond that. I think it had been about half a year since
the last letterboxer had signed in. The only reason we even found
it was because the 'stick altar' caught my eye as we were heading
off the trail to return back to our car--otherwise, we would never
have found it.

I was being a bit facetious when I said grid square (it's just a
term we use in the military)--I'm sure though that with the 100,000+
geocaches located around the world, that there may indeed be one
with nothing but a grid-square 'hint'--I just don't have the time to
peruse the list, though. ;)

GPSs are only as accurate as the number of satellite fixes you have
at any given time--the more fixes, the more precise your location on
the GPS, the fewer fixes, the less accurate. Satellites have
different coverage times throughout the day depending upon what
types of orbits they use and what their 'true' purposes are/what
company's equipment you're using, etc--so depending upon when one
group of folks visits an area and prints up a GPSr coordinate--it
may differ a bit from when the next visitor is in the area using a
different GPSr. Then of course there are different precision
levels, individual machine errors, and such. Sometimes in geocache
clues, there's an additional hint that one must decrypt in order to
get more precise info as to where a cache is located--usually it's
there to help limit the amount of environmental damage caused in a
specific area (i.e. if you know the cache is near a 9' tall boulder
then you won't look around the small pebbles or bushes but limit
your search to the boulders). In yesterday's case, the more
precise 'hint' was simply a link to the letterbox clue--even though
the actually box had been relocated some 30 steps away (60+ feet)
from its original hiding spot.

Just a different approach to a similar game--nothing more, nothing
less. By the way, our find yesterday was about our 70th geocache
find without using a GPS (no thanks extended to the jerk who broke
into our car at Pt Defiance Park and stole our GPS a few years ago)!
