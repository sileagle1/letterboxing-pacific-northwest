Re: publicity--your opinion, please

From: Mark (nomad_pnw@hotmail.com) | Date: 2003-07-21 22:47:42 UTC
In the beginning, I wasn't so sure that articles
of this nature were such a good idea, as drawing
attention to such an activity can sometimes have a
negative effect.

However, the story of Letterboxing has already been
written up many times before, and no doubt
will be again in the future. You certainly
wouldn't be sharing anything new that the
media hasn't already become aware of.

I'm a little surprised, though, that a "reporter"
wouldn't just go out on their own for a letterbox
adventure. On the other hand, the request to
tag along could work well for us, allowing us to
share some KEY INPUTS about Letterboxing, especially
for new folks becoming interested in the activity.

Since this "sport" has increased expedentially in
popularity within the last couple of years, perhaps
it's a good time to help educate those who are new to it
and refresh those that have been doing it for awhile too.

As far as the process goes to get a letterbox together, it isn't
rocket science. You carve (hopefully) a stamp, place it in a
container that seals well, include a small journal to record
visitors, publish clues in order to find the box and hide it
accordingly.

It's not the process that concerns me, instead its the responsibility
of the sport and those who take part in it. The key inputs I'm
talking about are things like "where to responsibly hide your
boxes", "what kind of ink works best (permanent ink vs. the other
kinds)", "why cleaning the stamp after using it makes sense and how
easy it is to do", "how to properly rehide the box so others can
share in the fun", "properly maintaining your boxes", "finding owners
to adopt them if you want out or can't properly maintain them", etc...

Like all good things, responsibility has to come into play
somewhere, or we are all just hiding little pieces of trash
all over our countryside, which will eventually create a negative
impression of what Letterboxing is really all about.

Take the subject of "where to hide boxes" for instance. Not many
folks I've spoken with about this subject care for it much. Perhaps
it is too convicting for them. But doesn't it make sense to hide our
boxes in a reasonable location, not in areas where it is not wanted
or difficult to keep concealed by its finders? Some examples include
hiding boxes on Private land, National Parks, private gardens or
land, caves, private establishments, environmental protected or
sensitive areas, etc...

I believe placing boxes in these kinds of areas can be okay "with
permission", but I'd bet most folks who place boxes in those areas
already know that if they had asked permission to put it there, the
answer would most likely have been "NO". So, they do it anyway
without asking.

What kind of example is that for our children, especially since so
many families enjoy this activity together? Aren't we demonstrating
that it's okay to ignore laws and put Letterboxes wherever we want,
just because it's a fun thing to do and we want it that way. How is
that really different than dumping our personal garbage wherever we
want?

Quickly fading are the days it seems of skillful written clues to
entice the seeker to find the Letterbox. Like in the days when Der
Mad Stamper was around, or Thom Powell, the pioneers of Letterboxing
in this area. Now days, it seems more important to put out as many
boxes as you can, just to improve your "P" count. Clues so simple
you can literally step out of your car, take 1-10 ten steps, turn
over the most obvious rock, and collect an image of a stamp
consisting of a few scratches on an eraser. Perhaps the "P" count
should refer to Pathetic, instead of Placed.


But alas, I am off on a tangent here. Still, I believe the points I
brought up needed to be made.

Back to the topic on the reporter, "yes", tell her about Letterboxing
and talk about the KEY INPUTS that will keep it a fun sport for a
long time to come.

Mark
